## Terms & Definitions

**Q:** What is **timbre**?  
**A:** What makes a sound distinct (tone colour / quality).

**Q:** What does **crooning** mean, and who exemplified it?  
**A:** Smooth, round, clear singing with little distortion; made possible by microphones. Doris Day is the example (vs. earlier “shouters” who had to be loud over the band).

**Q:** What is a **riff**?  
**A:** A short, self-contained musical gesture that repeats and becomes the basis for a longer piece. Today often called “beats.”

**Q:** What is **call and response**?  
**A:** Back-and-forth flow of information/energy between performer and audience; audience is as important as the performer.

**Q:** What does **AABA** mean in song form?  
**A:** A form where A is the main melody and B is a contrasting section; B should create tension so you want to return to A.

**Q:** What is **race music**?  
**A:** Category defined by intended (Black) audience; later renamed R&B. No rule about the race of the artist.

**Q:** What is **hillbilly** music?  
**A:** Category defined more by geography (rural) than race; later becomes country (and western).

**Q:** **Parlour songs** – what and for whom?  
**A:** Songs for middle-class women in the 1800s, linked to the industrial revolution; often piano or guitar.

**Q:** What is **TPA** / **Tin Pan Alley**?  
**A:** Place and idea (NYC, 1880s, Broadway area) where songwriting was standardized and commercialized; “tin pans” = pianos; division of labour, simple lyrics, AABA, middle-class audience.

**Q:** What are **African retentions**?  
**A:** Musical traits from West Africa that were carried over to North America through the slave trade (e.g. call and response, interest in distorted timbres, riffs).

**Q:** What is **jump blues**?  
**A:** Faster than country blues, same “jump” feel; faster dancing; e.g. “Good Rockin' Tonight,” Wynonie Harris.

**Q:** **Spiritual** vs **gospel** (by date)?  
**A:** Before 1865 → spiritual; after 1865 → gospel. Both worship music from African American culture.

**Q:** What is **78 rpm**?  
**A:** Standard early record speed; a compromise between storage and sound quality; allowed records to be interchangeable.

**Q:** What is **Top 40 radio**?  
**A:** Format based on the top 40 most popular songs from jukeboxes, played repeatedly; 40 was seen as the optimal number.

**Q:** What does **“floating pool of verses”** mean in work songs and blues?  
**A:** Verses that could be mixed and matched; leaders could improvise or plug in local events; not one fixed “song.”

## Big Band

**Q:** How many musicians in a typical big band?  
**A:** About 15–20.

**Q:** Name the four main sections of a big band.  
**A:** Front (4–5 saxes), middle (3–4 trombones), back (4–5 trumpets), side/rhythm (drummer, bass, guitar, piano); plus vocalist.

**Q:** In big band era, who was the “star” – vocalist or bandleader?  
**A:** Bandleader (e.g. Les Brown); vocalists were part of the band, not the main focus.

**Q:** What year was “Sentimental Journey” and who was the vocalist?  
**A:** 1944; Doris Day (with Les Brown’s band); most popular song of 1945.

## Industry & Technology

**Q:** When did copyright start to cover sheet music (US)?  
**A:** 1790s–1830s (amendments to copyright law).

**Q:** Why did the record industry get into trouble around 1922?  
**A:** Radio offered “unlimited” content; people got tired of records quickly and liked the variety.

**Q:** Who brought Mamie Smith to record “Crazy Blues” and why did it matter?  
**A:** Perry Bradford; showed that Black vocalists could sell records and that there was a large Black market.

**Q:** Who coined the terms “race” and “hillbilly” for music?  
**A:** Ralph Peer.

**Q:** What was important about Memphis station WDIA?  
**A:** Switched to “race” records, reached Black communities, helped spread race music (broadcasting); young Elvis listened.

**Q:** What changed when soap operas moved from radio to TV?  
**A:** Radio started playing **records** and became more about music.

**Q:** Major vs independent record labels (main difference)?  
**A:** Major = own distribution network; independent = smaller, often rely on majors to distribute big hits.

## Songs – Writer / Performer / Date

**Q:** “Sentimental Journey” – writers and vocalist?  
**A:** Brown / Homer / Green; vocalist Doris Day (1944).

**Q:** “Good Rockin' Tonight” – who wrote it, who made it famous?  
**A:** Roy Brown wrote it; Wynonie Harris performed it (1947); jump blues.

**Q:** “Blue Moon of Kentucky” – who and what genre?  
**A:** Bill Monroe, 1946/1947; hillbilly.

**Q:** “After The Ball” – who wrote it and why is it important?  
**A:** Charles K. Harris, 1891; made him one of the wealthiest men in America; late parlour song.

**Q:** “Somewhere Over The Rainbow” – writers and performer?  
**A:** Arlen / Harburg; Judy Garland, Wizard of Oz (1939).

**Q:** “Crazy Blues” – writer and performer and year?  
**A:** Perry Bradford (writer), Mamie Smith (performer), 1920; first major race record hit.

**Q:** “Travelin' Blues” – who and when?  
**A:** Blind Willie McTell, 1929; country blues.

**Q:** “Sweet Home Chicago” – who and when?  
**A:** Robert Johnson, 1936; country blues, start of race music being taken more seriously.

**Q:** “Carve that Possum” – who and when?  
**A:** Uncle Dave Macon and the Fruit Jar Drinkers, 1927; hillbilly.

**Q:** “How Long Blues” – who and when?  
**A:** Carr and Blackwell, 1928; urban/city blues, piano + guitar.

**Q:** “Too Young” – who performed it and what year was it most popular?  
**A:** Nat King Cole; most popular song of 1951 (writers Lippman, Dee).

**Q:** “(How Much Is) That Doggie in the Window” – who and why notable?  
**A:** Patti Page, 1952 (Merril writer); early overdubbing; most popular 1953.

**Q:** “I've Got A Woman” – who and what style?  
**A:** Ray Charles, 1954; gospel-influenced R&B; Atlantic Records.

**Q:** “Hoochie Coochie Man” – writer and performer?  
**A:** Willie Dixon (writer); Muddy Waters (performer), 1954; Chicago electric blues, Chess Records.

## People

**Q:** Robert Johnson – why “mythic”?  
**A:** Story that he disappeared 18 months and sold his soul to the devil to be the best guitarist; in reality he died young (poisoned) and likely studied guitar in Memphis.

**Q:** Why was Ray Charles able to reach white audiences?  
**A:** He was on Atlantic Records, a **major** label with strong distribution and promotion, unlike many R&B acts on independents.

**Q:** What did Muddy Waters do when he moved to the city?  
**A:** Switched from acoustic to **electric** guitar to be heard in louder urban settings; part of Chicago blues.

**Q:** What is distinctive about Bill Monroe’s vocals in “Blue Moon of Kentucky”?  
**A:** Resonating in the sinuses; emotionally distant – “sing but don’t overly emote.”

## Dates & Eras

**Q:** When did blues appear vs when was it first recorded?  
**A:** Blues appears in the 1890s; first recorded in the 1920s (labels focused on white/pop music before that).

**Q:** Same question for parlour/industrial era sheet music?  
**A:** Sheet music selling took off with industrial revolution; copyright for sheet music in US in 1790s–1830s.

**Q:** What is the “arc of popularity”?  
**A:** The idea that every style and artist goes through a rise and fall in popularity.

## One-line review

**Q:** Hillbilly → ? Race → ?  
**A:** Hillbilly → country (and western). Race → R&B.

**Q:** Work songs – role of the song leader?  
**A:** Led call-and-response, set pace, could look out for weaker workers, used “floating pool” of verses.

**Q:** Why did guitar become more important in city blues?  
**A:** Playing with others (e.g. piano) freed the guitarist to take solos; later amplifiers made guitar loud enough for cities and created distortion.

**Q:** What did amplifiers do for guitar in blues?  
**A:** Made it loud enough for cities; players found that turning amps up past “normal” created distortion, which linked to West African interest in distorted timbres.
