# Master questions (lec 1–7 + tech / rock ’n’ roll)

Q1. What is timbre, and why is it important in understanding popular music styles?

Q2. In the mid‑1940s, what was the big band format (size and sections), and what was its primary purpose?

Q3. How did the role of the vocalist in big bands differ from the role of vocalists in later popular music?

Q4. What is crooning, and what technological change made it possible?

Q5. In the basic 1945 category system, who were the intended audiences for Pop, Race, and Hillbilly music?

Q6. Why are Pop, Race, and Hillbilly best understood as categories defined by intended audience rather than the race of the performer?

Q7. What social and economic changes around 1890 made “After the Ball” the first “hit song” on sheet music?

Q8. What is a Victorian parlour song, and what values of Victorian middle‑class society did it express?

Q9. How did the Industrial Revolution and the rise of the middle class shape the market for sheet music?

Q10. What is Tin Pan Alley (TPA), where was it located, and what is meant by its division of labour?

Q11. Describe the typical TPA lyrical style (themes and complexity) and explain why it was written that way.

Q12. What is AABA song form, and how does the B section function musically?

Q13. In early blues, what is meant by a “floating pool of verse”, and how did it relate to the performer’s social role?

Q14. What are the main musical features of country (rural) blues (instrumentation, bar structure, lyric pattern, and texture)?

Q15. How does Robert Johnson illustrate the growing self‑consciousness of blues musicians compared to earlier folk blues performers?

Q16. What role did work songs play for enslaved people, and how did the song leader function within them?

Q17. List and briefly define the major African retentions that influenced North American popular music (e.g., percussion, distortion, riff, call‑and‑response).

Q18. How did the shift from cylinders to discs and the standardization of 78 rpm change the recording industry?

Q19. Why did early record companies initially focus on TPA/pop instead of Race and Hillbilly recordings?

Q20. Why was Mamie Smith’s “Crazy Blues” (1920) so important for the recording industry?

Q21. How and why did the rise of commercial radio almost destroy the record industry in the early 1920s?

Q22. What was WDIA (Memphis, 1948), and why was it important for the spread of Race music?

Q23. What post‑war economic and social conditions in North America created the environment for “safe” pop music like Nat King Cole and Patti Page?

Q24. How did the development of the concept of the “teenager” after WWII change the music market?

Q25. Define Jump Blues: where did it come from, what did the typical ensemble look like, and what was its performance focus?

Q26. How did Ray Charles blend gospel and secular music, and why was his success on a major label significant?

Q27. What were the key musical innovations and sonic characteristics of Chicago electric blues (e.g., Muddy Waters, “Hoochie Coochie Man”)?

Q28. What is a disc jockey (DJ), why was the term originally an insult, and what problem did DJs solve for radio stations?

Q29. How did Top 40 radio develop, and why was the “40” significant?

Q30. What are cover versions in the 1954–1956 context, and why were race‑based covers of R&B songs controversial?

Q31. Using “Tutti Frutti” and “Sh‑Boom” as examples, how did white cover versions change the sound, form, and sexuality of the originals?

Q32. What is a crossover hit, and how did black appeal radio contribute to crossovers between Pop, R&B, and Country & Western charts?

Q33. What is meant by “moral panic” around early rock and roll, and why did some white parents object to their kids listening to R&B?

Q34. How is Rock ’n’ Roll defined in this course (stylistic influences and target audience), and where does the term come from culturally?

Q35. Who was Bill Haley, what is his birth year (test detail), and why did his age become a limitation as rock and roll grew?

Q36. What are the key musical features and historical circumstances of “Rock Around the Clock” (style, form, film connection, chart success)?

Q37. Summarize Sam Phillips’ role at Sun Records and his search for a “white man with the voice of a black man.”

Q38. Describe Elvis Presley’s early background (birthplace, musical influences, churches, radio listening) and how this shaped his style.

Q39. What happened at Elvis’s first Sun session with Scotty Moore and Bill Black that led to “That’s Alright Mama” being recorded?

Q40. How does Elvis’s 1954 performance of “Blue Moon of Kentucky” differ from Bill Monroe’s original in terms of meter, feel, and vocal approach?

Q41. What is rockabilly, and why is Elvis called the “Hillbilly Cat”?

Q42. Why did Sam Phillips sell Elvis’s contract to RCA, and what did Colonel Tom Parker do for Elvis in 1956?

Q43. What were the chart achievements of “Heartbreak Hotel”, “Hound Dog”, and “Don’t Be Cruel”, and why are they historically important?

Q44. What happened during Elvis’s performances of “Hound Dog” on Texaco Star Theatre and The Steve Allen Show, and how did these relate to moral panic?

Q45. Outline the main phases of Elvis’s career trajectory from Sun Records through the army, Hollywood movies, the 1968 Comeback Special, Las Vegas residency, and his death.

Q46. What key biographical facts and motivations distinguish Chuck Berry from Elvis (background, guitar focus, desire to be rich)?

Q47. How did Chuck Berry’s strategy at Chess Records (rewriting “Ida Red” into “Maybellene”) reflect both business sense and musical innovation?

Q48. What are the three core themes of Rock ’n’ Roll identified in the course, and how do they appear in Berry’s songs?

Q49. Using “Johnny B. Goode” as a case study, what are the white elements and black elements in Berry’s style?

Q50. Why did bands in the 1960s (e.g., the Beatles) model themselves more on Chuck Berry than on Elvis?

Q51. What three quantitative measures define the Golden Age of Rock and Roll (1954–1959)?

Q52. What was the Payola scandal, how did major labels use it against rock and roll, and what happened to Alan Freed?

Q53. How was opposition to rock and roll tied to racism and the emergence of the Civil Rights Movement (Brown v. Board, Rosa Parks, Little Rock Nine, etc.)?

Q54. What is the Great Extinction (1957–1959), and what happened to each of the major rock and roll stars named in lecture?

Q55. What defines the In‑Between Years (1959–1963), and why did major labels turn to sub‑genres like the Dance Craze and Teen Idols?

Q56. What are the key features of the Dance Craze (e.g., “The Twist,” “The Locomotion”) and how do they reflect division of labour?

Q57. What were Teen Idols, and how did songs like “Blue Velvet” function in terms of style, lyrics, and target demographic?

Q58. What was the Brill Building, what was Aldon Music, and how was this environment similar to classic Tin Pan Alley?

Q59. Who was Jack Mullin, what was the Magnetophone, and how did Bing Crosby change radio recording practices using tape?

Q60. Who was Les Paul, and what were his two major contributions to popular music technology?

Q61. Define multitrack recording and explain how it transformed recording from reproduction to production.

Q62. What is the role of the producer before and after the in‑between years, and how did Phil Spector’s “Wall of Sound” exemplify the new role?

Q63. What is verse/chorus form, how does it differ from AABA, and what is an important early example discussed in lecture?

Q64. What are Girl Groups and Boy Bands in this course’s terms, and how do they relate to Brill‑Building‑style production?

Q65. What defines Surf Music musically and culturally (instrumentation, electric bass/guitar, youth culture, geography)?

Q66. Who are Dick Dale and The Beach Boys, and how does “Surfin’ USA” show the influence of Chuck Berry?

Q67. Who is Brian Wilson, and why is he considered so important musically and as a producer?

Q68. How did post‑war Britain’s economic situation lead to DIY skiffle culture and ultimately help launch the Beatles?

Q69. What is skiffle, and how does Lonnie Donegan’s “Rock Island Line” illustrate it?

Q70. Outline the early career path of the Beatles from The Quarrymen to Hamburg, the Cavern Club, Brian Epstein, and finally their Parlophone/EMI contract.

Q71. Why did DECA Records initially reject the Beatles, and what does this tell us about the state of the music industry around 1962?

Q72. Who was George Martin, and what skills did he bring from comedy records to producing the Beatles?

Q73. In “Please Please Me”, what formal and performance details (form, vocal density, B‑section climax) show the Beatles’ growing sophistication?
