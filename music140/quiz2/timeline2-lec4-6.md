## 1954

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| May 1954 | Brown v. Board of Education — Supreme Court rules segregated schools unconstitutional. Happens just *one month* before Elvis goes into Sun Records — not a coincidence; the same social shifts making both possible | Lec 5 |
| June 1954 | "Sh-Boom" — The Chords (Cat Records, independent label); #3 R&B, #5 Pop. Specifically a doowop song — relies heavily on background singers using nonsense syllables. Sense of intimacy: you can hear the singer breathing, which parents found provocative | Lec 4 |
| June 1954 | "Sh-Boom" cover — The Crew Cuts (major label); #1 Pop for 9 weeks. Key differences: no breathing/intimacy, farther from mic, restrained vocal timbre, uses a timpani (classical instrument, lowers "threat level" vs. sax solo), AABA structure emphasised over 12-bar blues. The Chords' original *disappeared* from public consciousness for decades | Lec 4 |
| July 1954 | Elvis Presley starts recording with Scotty Moore (guitar) and Bill Black (double bass) at Sun Records. Session goes badly — Sam still mentally places Elvis in C&W where drums were non-standard, so no drummer is present | Lec 4 |
| July 1954 | During a break in a failed session, Elvis spontaneously starts playing "That's Alright Mama" (an R&B song). Sam hears it, recognises the cultural blending, and rushes a copy to DJ Dewey Phillips | Lec 4 |
| July 1954 | Dewey Phillips plays "That's Alright Mama" on the radio — phones go crazy; he plays it 13–17 times in two hours | Lec 4 |
| July 1954 | Next evening: Elvis goes to the station for an interview. First question asked: "Where do you go to school?" — a coded way to reveal his race (Memphis was segregated, only 2 high schools). Listeners were stunned to learn he was white | Lec 4 + missing details |
| 1954 | "Blue Moon of Kentucky" — Elvis Presley's reworking. Original was a waltz (3/4); Elvis converts to 4/4, making it a pop song. He sings it with energy despite the sad lyrics — "the words now mean nothing," meaning is carried entirely by the music. Voice is percussive and distorted; slap echo added; Bill Black uses slap bass for rhythmic drive (no drummer) | Lec 4 |
| 1954 | "Rock Around the Clock" — Bill Haley and the Comets (Freedman/Myers); recorded this year but popularity comes in 1955 | Lec 4 |
| 1954 | DJ Fontana added as drummer to Elvis's group | Lec 4 notes |
| 1954 | Elvis's early style coined "Rockabilly" — a blend of Rock and Roll and Hillbilly. Nickname: "The Hillbilly Cat" — "Hillbilly" = western/country, "Cat" = Black slang for someone cool and in the know | Lec 4 + missing details |
| 1954 | Elvis makes 6 records (12 songs), all covers — one side R&B, one side C&W — both pulled toward a new blended middle style. Becomes a regional star | Lec 4 notes |

## 1955

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1955 | "Rock Around the Clock" rises to national popularity after being featured in the film *Blackboard Jungle* (about rowdy teenagers) — used to amplify moral panic. Bill Haley is now considered "old" (30 years old) by the teen audience | Lec 4 |
| 1955 | "Tutti Frutti" — Little Richard (Penniman, LaBostrie); #2 R&B, #17 Pop. Released on Specialty Records (independent, small label). Lyrics were revised (from sexually explicit originals) by Dorothy LaBostrie. Uses stop-time and a modified 12-bar blues | Lec 4 |
| July 1955 | Chuck Berry signs with Chess Records. He plays a C&W song ("Ida Red") instead of R&B to stand out. Chess brothers point out he doesn't own the song — so he rewrites it to own the copyright and keep all the money | Lec 5 |
| 1955 | "Maybellene" — Chuck Berry (rewritten from "Ida Red"); #1 R&B, #5 Pop. Chuck invented a fake backstory for the name (from a makeup bag) to avoid a copyright lawsuit — money-motivated as always | Lec 5 + missing details |
| December 1955 | Rosa Parks refuses to give up her bus seat — she wasn't deliberately sitting in the "wrong" section; she was in her *correct* section and simply refused when another passenger demanded her seat. Triggers the Montgomery Bus Boycott | Lec 5 + missing details |

## 1956

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1956 | "Tutti Frutti" cover — Pat Boone (major label); #10 Pop, no R&B chart presence. Tamer vocal timbre, more restrained | Lec 4 |
| 1956 | Race-based cover versions stop being profitable — the financial motivation disappears, and so does the practice | Lec 4 |
| 1956 | Sam Phillips sells Elvis's contract to Colonel Tom Parker / RCA for $35,000 — at the time the most money ever paid for a contract transfer. Sam uses this money to fund the careers of artists like Johnny Cash | Lec 5 + missing details |
| 1956 | Colonel Tom Parker puts Elvis on national TV 11 times — few channels, watched by tens of millions each time. Elvis holds #1 on the Pop charts for 30 weeks — not just individual songs but sustained total dominance | Lec 5 + missing details |
| 1956 | Elvis's 1956 success is part of the reason cover versions stop working — the original artists are now getting through to white audiences themselves | Lec 5 + missing details |
| 1956 | With RCA, Elvis now has access to professional TPA songwriters (division of labour system) | Lec 5 |
| April–May 1956 | "Heartbreak Hotel" — Elvis Presley; #1 Pop, #5 R&B | Lec 4 notes |
| June 1956 | Elvis performs "Hound Dog" on Texaco Star Theatre — goes overtime, confuses the band, loses control of the performance. Generates 10,000 letters of outrage about his provocative movements and perceived moral corruption of youth | Lec 4 notes |
| ~July 1956 (two weeks later) | Elvis performs "Hound Dog" on The Steve Allen Show — Tom Parker arranged this to calm the public. Elvis found it humiliating but had no choice | Lec 4 notes |
| Aug–Dec 1956 | "Hound Dog," "Don't Be Cruel," "Love Me Tender" — "Hound Dog" and "Don't Be Cruel" both chart #1 simultaneously on Pop, Country, and R&B (for at least one week) | Lec 4 notes |
| 1956 | "Peggy Sue" — Buddy Holly | Lec 5 notes |

## 1957

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1957 | The Little Rock Nine — first Black students to integrate a white school in Little Rock, Arkansas. The Governor originally called the National Guard to *block* students from entering. President Eisenhower was outraged and overruled him, commanding the National Guard to *escort* the students in instead | Lec 5 + missing details |
| After 1957 | Elvis stops charting on R&B; Colonel Tom Parker steers his career entirely toward mainstream Pop | Lec 4 notes |
| October 1957 | Little Richard quits rock and roll after a plane engine explodes mid-flight; he prays and interprets surviving as a sign — becomes a minister and leaves music | Lec 5 notes |

## 1958

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1958 | "Great Balls of Fire" — Jerry Lee Lewis (song is about literal great balls of fire; he came from the poor rural South) | Lec 5 notes |
| 1958 | "Johnny B. Goode" — Chuck Berry; #2 R&B, #8 Pop. Uses 12-bar blues, text-heavy narrative, clearly enunciated lyrics (influenced by Nat King Cole), country guitar sound (twangier/brighter than R&B), and boogie-woogie "added 6th." Guitar solo experiments with timbre by playing the same note in multiple positions on the neck. Fun fact: encoded on the Voyager spacecraft gold record | Lec 5 |
| March 1958 | Elvis Presley drafted into the U.S. Army (draft was still active post-WWII/Korean War era). Goes to Germany. Colonel Tom Parker had pre-recorded many singles to release during his absence | Lec 5 |
| May 1958 | Jerry Lee Lewis scandal: his marriage to his 13-year-old cousin (also not his first marriage) becomes public knowledge. Sun Records drops him. Career effectively ends | Lec 5 notes |

## 1959

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| February 1959 | "The Day the Music Died" — Buddy Holly, Ritchie Valens, and The Big Bopper (J.P. Richardson) all die in a plane crash after their bus broke down and one band member suffered frostbite. Later immortalised by Don McLean's "American Pie." Buddy Holly had joined DECA then left for an independent label | Lec 5 notes |
| December 1959 | Chuck Berry arrested under the Mann Act — he had transported a young woman (who worked as a hostess in his nightclub) across the Mississippi River (a state line) for illegal purposes. He lived on the St. Louis side, west of the river | Lec 5 notes |
| 1959 | End of the Golden Age of Rock and Roll (1954–1959) — measured by: industry worth growing from $200M (1954) to $600M (1959); Rock and Roll's share of the Pop Top 10 growing from 15% (1955) to 42% (1959); independent label share of the music industry growing from 21% (1955) to 66% (1959) | Lec 5 |

## 1960–1963 (The In-Between Years)

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| March 1960 | Elvis returns from the Army | Lec 5 |
| March 1961 | Elvis's last live performance for seven years — Colonel Tom Parker transitions him entirely to movies. Films follow a repeating formula: small town with a problem → Elvis solves it → falls in love → finds an acoustic guitar and sings → soundtrack album generates hits. Quality declines with overproduction | Lec 5 + missing details |
| 1961 | "The Twist" — Chubby Checker (written by Hank Ballard); part of the Dance Craze era. Major labels fill the gap left by the Great Extinction by promoting sub-genres that appeal to teens while fitting the Division of Labour system | Lec 6 |
| 1962 | "The Locomotion" — Little Eva (written by Goffin & Carole King); Eva Boyd was Carole King's babysitter. Heavily features background singing — considered the OG of that style. Another Dance Craze era song | Lec 6 |
| 1963 | "Blue Velvet" — Bobby Vinton; the song was originally composed in 1950 and was a TPA hit for Tony Bennett in 1951. Vinton's cover brings back TPA-style sound for the teen market — Teen Idol strategy by major labels (clean-cut, idealized, non-threatening romance) | Lec 6 |
| 1963 | "Be My Baby" — The Ronettes (Barry, Greenwich, Spector). Produced by Phil Spector using his "Wall of Sound" technique: layering enormous numbers of instruments so densely that you can't identify individual parts — "a musical stew." Uses verse/chorus form, which becomes increasingly dominant from this point on | Lec 6 |

## Technology Sub-Timeline (Lecture 6)

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| WWII era | Nazi Germany invents the Magnetophone (first tape-to-tape recorder). Jack Mullin (US Army engineer) discovers it on his way to England | Lec 6 |
| Post-WWII | Jack Mullin demonstrates the Magnetophone to a public audience by playing a string classical recording; the sound quality shocks everyone | Lec 6 |
| ~1947–48 | Bing Crosby (major 30s–50s star with a live weekly radio show) adopts the Magnetophone to pre-record broadcasts — you can now cut and edit tape to improve recordings. Jack Mullin becomes his recording assistant | Lec 6 |
| 1948 | Bing Crosby creates AMPEX — one of the world's biggest tape manufacturers. Same year, gives one of the first commercially produced reel-to-reel recorders to Les Paul | Lec 6 |
| 1948–1953 | Les Paul (1915–2009) experiments with multitrack recording: splits tape into two sections, records independent streams of audio (layering). He adjusts equalization, tone, volume and layers up to 4–8 tracks. Also discovers varied-speed recordings (making sounds humans can't physically produce). Simultaneously builds "the Log" — a solid-body electric guitar out of solid wood, later commercialised as the Gibson Les Paul | Lec 6 |
| 1953 | "Sitting On Top of the World" — Les Paul with Mary Ford. All instruments by Les Paul, all vocals by Mary Ford. Recorded over several days in layers. A landmark showcase of multitrack recording — shifts recording from *reproduction* to *production* | Lec 6 |
| In-Between Years | Phil Spector develops the "Wall of Sound" producing style: layers dozens of instruments, records them all first, then records vocals on top. His instrument is the modern soundboard | Lec 6 |
| 1963 | "Be My Baby" — The Ronettes; the definitive Wall of Sound example | Lec 6 |
