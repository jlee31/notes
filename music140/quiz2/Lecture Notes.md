### North American Popular Music – 1945
Music scene was much like today with a few big artists and genres and old ones being phased out. 

By 1945:  
- end of the “Big Band” era.  
- Big Bands had dominated music industry since mid 1930s.

Big bands (E.g. Perdedo):
- 15-20 musicians
- 4 distinct sessions
	- Front: 4-5 saxophones
	- Middle: 3 - 4 Trombones
	- Back: 4-5 Trumpet
	- Side: Rhythm Section
		- Drummer
		- Bass Player
			- Double bass
		- Guitar
		- Piano
	- Vocalist
		- Were not the stars, just part of the band
		- Much of the musi was instrumental, music since has become more focused on the vocalist
- The music was intended for dancing
	- Notice the feel and groove, the rhythm is conducive for dancing
- Although it was phased out in the 40s, this type of music still lives on through things like jazz ensembles in school
	- Nothing ever goes away completely
- Declined for many reasons, one being that they are big and it's hard to keep many people together
- Bangs transitioned to be smaller and the vocalists become the stars (still this way today)

## Work Songs
AUDIO EXAMPLE: “Old Alabama” 1947
- Given this name but technically never had one
- The songs were improvised
- Pass time / set pace, coordinate work.
- Can be thought of as a game:
	- Song leader role was very valuable, carried around many songs "Floating pool of verses"
	- The individual were very valuable slaves as they increase productivity
	- Each "component" had two lines, the leader would sing the first line, the rest of the group jumps in when they figure out what he's singing
	- The leader either chooses to sing the first line or move on, and the group needs to jump in when they figure it out
	- The leader then chooses a new set of two lines
- Song leader would look out for weaker or sick members of the group, setting the pace so everyone seems to be pulling their weight
- Work songs are a type of fold music

## The Blues
- Appears in the 1890s, but no one bothers to record it until the 1920s (recording technology existed but the owners were focused on white music)
- Country (Rural) Blues:
	- Not to be confused with Hillbilly
	- Lone wandering and the danger associated contributed to the male dominance of the music
	- Acoustic guitar was light and suited for travelling
	- Early blues had a more nasal vocal sound before it transitioned
	- Putting voice to their troubles
- Musical Form:
	- "Floating Pool of verse":
		- Artists would sometimes arrive at a town, learn about whats going on, and incorporate these events into their verses

Musical Form Characteristics:  
- 12 bar phrases (the terms ‘bars’ and ‘measures’ is interchangeable).  
- a-a-b lyric pattern (Lower-case letters for words)  
- call and response between guitar and voice  
- early Blues performers made use of “Floating pool of verse”
- See chart on lecture overhead

## Changes in Technology
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonograph#Early_history
- 1877 - Thomas Edison invents the phonograph (gramophone)
- 1887 – transition from cylinder to disks begins
- 1910 – disks become dominant medium
- Early 1910s – disk speed is standardized 78 rpm

- The delicate early cylinders prevented the idea of home adoption and was mostly a technical marvel
- Disks were much more durable, having two sides gave much more room for recording
- The technology transition into normal life was slow like most new technologies
- Phonograph and gramophone can be used interchangeably, gramophone was just a brand name (nothing to do with cylinder vs disk)
- 78 rpm:
	- Compromise between storage and quality
	- Allowed more records to be sold as they could be used interchangeably 
### What was recorded?
- Sheet music / TPA. Early record industry controlled by the same people who controlled TPA.
- Early TPA members saw records as a way to advertise sheet music 
	- Playing songs in stores
	- One reason why it wasn't seen as worth recording race and hillbilly music, there would be no sales to people in the countryside
- Cultural chauvinism was the main reason only pop music was recorded, poor and black were looked down on 
	- generally, not part of the TPA/sheet music industry
### Commercial Radio
- Everything that would've ended up on T.V was broadcast-ed through radio (cooking shows, children shows, etc.)

- The success of radio almost ruined the record industry, very serious trouble by 1922
	- People got tired of records fast, not like today where people get very attached, so the idea of unlimited content was very appealing
