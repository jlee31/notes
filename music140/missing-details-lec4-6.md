## Lecture 4 — Additions & Clarifications

| When | What You Missed |
|------|----------------|
| Context | **Top 40** — 40 was deemed the *optimal* number of songs for jukebox rotation, not arbitrary |
| Context | **Cover versions** — the practice wasn't new; the idea of "owning" a song didn't really exist yet. The *pattern* of white covers of black music is what made it problematic |
| Context | **Cover versions motivation** — major labels were purely chasing profit (white wealthy audiences had more money), not always intentionally racist |
| June 1954 | **"Sh-Boom" (The Chords)** — specifically a **doowop** song, not just R&B. Doowop relied heavily on background singers using nonsense syllables |
| Post-1954 | The original **Chords version of "Sh-Boom" disappeared** from public consciousness due to the Crew Cuts cover — until **Pixar's *Cars* (2006)** revived it. Now the original is the one most people know |
| 1954 | Elvis's first Sun Records session had **no drummer** because Sam Phillips still mentally placed Elvis in C&W, where drums weren't standard. The whole recording session was going *badly* until Elvis spontaneously started playing "That's Alright Mama" on break |
| July 1954 | After Dewey Phillips played "That's Alright Mama," Elvis went to the station for an interview. The **first question asked was "where do you go to school?"** — a coded way to reveal his race without asking directly (Memphis had segregated schools, only 2 high schools) |
| 1954–1955 | **"Hillbilly Cat"** nickname — "Hillbilly" = western/country; **"Cat"** = Black slang for *someone cool and in the know* |
| Context | Elvis's covers were **different in motivation** from the racist cover versions — he wasn't trying to sanitize Black music, he was genuinely blending cultures he grew up with |
