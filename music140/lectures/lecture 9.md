# Soul and Funk (Returning to the USA in the 1960s)
- Blues music falls out of favour because something new came along : Soul music
- Closely linked to African American Identity
	- Development connected to the Civil Rights movement and the development of African American identity.
	- Star Trek is the only hour of TV where they can see a black woman doing something incredible
	- It was a time where African Americans are still heavily marginalized , so these small moments matter a lot
- Late 1950s – growing anticipation of civil rights
- Rejection of blues as the sound of the past, rural south, slavery.
- New sound of the urban - Soul Music
#### SOUL MUSIC IS A FUSION OF:
1. Vocal style from Gospel
	1. Adding extra notes
	2. Big Jumps in Range
	3. Shows power of singers
2. Rhythm beat of R&B
	1. Music intended for dancing / celebratory 
3. Arrangements and lyric styles from TPA
	1. Songs were simple
	2. Lyrics were about adult relationships

Motown - Detroit
	- "Hitsville" USA
	- Founded by Berry Gordy
	- Starts in 1959 – Based on Gordy’s experience in automotive plants  
		- Assembly Line – Everything under one roof
		- Like TPA, but literally everything in a single company, and the target market would be for black artists targeting a black audience (not done before)
	- MOTOR CITY -> MOTOWN
	- song writers: Holland/Dozier/Holland, Smokey Robinson  
	- Maxine Powell: Ran a local Finishing School (meant for teaching upper class women on how to be a housewife)
		- Gordy wanted to target a black audience but not exclusively
		- Maxine would teach them how to speak for a white audience in interviews and media
	- Cholly Atkins: Choreographer  
		- More "proper" dances
		- Gordy wants more control and restraint
		- Small tightly controlled movements
	- The Funk Brothers: House band
		- Loose collective of Detroit's best musicians
		- Would work for Motown from 12-5 and do their own gigs in the evening
 		- ~20 all together, not a fixed band
		- James Jammerson was a key figure, anchor of the band
	- Result is absolute consistency of product
		- Only the singers change, the rest of the assembly line is the same
	- Smokey and the Miracles: “You’d Better Shop Around” (Robinson, Gordy, 1960)
		- Robinson (an important songwriter for other artists as well) is performing here
		- Clear TPA performance
			- Polished, restrained presentation – sophistication
			- Complexity in background vocals (not in R&B)
			- Idealized romance in the lyrics
				- Looking for love, going to mother for advice
		- Choreography will small controlled movements
		- Gospel vocal influence
			- Ornamentation in his voice
			- Vocal acrobatics, big jump up with rapid fall
		- Lip syncing allowed for more consistent performance, controlled sound, also controls the performance (band can't go off book, remember the fear of Elvis Presley)
			- Also need fewer people on stage
	- Soul music is very focused on singers, not on bands
		- It will never go down the road of bands
	- The Supremes: EXAMPLE: “Stop in the Name of Love” (Holland/Dozier/Holland 1965)
		- Strong dance beat  
		- Lyrics-idealized romance  
		- Detailed arrangement (Vibraphone)
			- Vibraphone: Motown songs often included something that brought a uniqueness to the sound
			- Gospel Influence: Both piano and organ (common is soul music)
		- Less gospel influence (Diana Ross)
			- One reason was that Gordy was having an affair with Diano Ross and that was the reason she was the lead singer
			- Another reason was that Gordy wanted to be successful (Hitsville) and was hoping her softer voice would appeal to a wider fanbase

- Stevie Wonder - came from Motown

- Motown
	- Focussed on the clarity of the sound

	- Berry Gordy was the main control guy 
		- Constantly looking for ways to make his songs a hit

Otis Redding:  
- Was a song writer and performer himself

- “Try A Little Tenderness”: (1966)  
	- Stax reworked the song
	- TPA song written in the 1920s  
	- Hit for Bing Crosby in 1933  
	- Unrestrained energy in performance
	- No background vocals
	- No background performance, just Redding dancing
	- People like Smokey Robinson always looked towards the audience
	- Otis Redding would close his eyes while he sings
	- Motown artists would look towards the audience (they were trained to)
	- Stax artists would just do whatever they did
	- Motown - being classy 
	- Stax - letting people do what they want (not get in the way)
	- Died in 1967 (At 27) due to plane crash

Sound and Production Practice
- Stax did not use of multitrack recording  
- They focus was to do everything as a group (monophonic recordings)
- Collective decision making (checked to see which recording is best)
- Less emphasis on arrangements than Motown.  
- Focus on energy of performance over accuracy.

- Example:
	- Sam and Dave:  “Soul Man” 1967
	- Great groove, great intensity
	- Less arrangement / instruments than Motown
	- Little background vocals
	- The Horn players forgot to play 
	- The energy was the most important
