Hard Rock / Heavy Metal

Shift to the “Hippie Aesthetic”
            - started by The Beatles’ Sgt. Peppers
            - marks the shift from “Rock ‘n’ Roll” to “Rock” - moving from:
                   o singles to albums o dancing to listening
                   o entertainers to “serious” musicians
            - sonic development from Psychedelic Blues / British Blues Revival
            - to a great extent it is a response to the failure of Counter Culture
            - The philosophy of the counter culture was more focused on the advantages of the collective.
            With the shootings at Kent State, the philosophy of the collective began to give way to more of a
            focus on the individual.

While the lyrics and themes of Hard Rock / Heavy Metal are quite different from those of the counter culture, it
was very much made possible by the changes that take place in the late 1960s – particularly those connected
with The Beatles and the shift to the ‘Hippie Aesthetic.” This new style would be very much focused on albums
and listening rather than singles and dancing. And there will be a significant focus on artistry and musicianship.
The sound of HR/HM will be influenced by important elements of the mid to late 1960s such as Psychedelic
Blues (Jimi Hendrix) and the British Blues Revival, but there will be differences in terms of how the influences
are found. However, in terms of lyrics and themes, HR/HM is very much a response to the failure of the
counter-culture turning away from the collective to focus on the individual. The events of the 1970s discussed
above, will push culture even more towards the individual.

Hard Rock and Heavy Metal are often considered two different styles and there are significant distinctions. But
it makes more sense to consider them as extremes of a continuum (HR at one end, HM at the other). Some
bands will be clearly rooted in one end or the other, but many bands will fall in-between, exhibiting
characteristics from both ends of the scale. Also, the characteristics will evolve over time and so a group that
may have been considered HM at one time, may now be considered HR because of more recent stylistic
changes.
So, if we put the two styles on a scale, what will distinguish one end from the other?

Distinctions between Hard Rock and Heavy Metal:
 1/ The sound of the distorted electric guitar is central to both, but Heavy Metal tends to have a much higher
level of distortion, and distortion is found not just in the guitar, but the bass and even the vocals.

2/ In keeping with the “Hippie Aesthetic” both styles put an emphasis on the technical ability of the musicians,
however Heavy Metal tends to be more concerned with complex musicianship.
Hard Rock songs are often performed at “mid-tempo.” While they still fall into what we have been describing
as the “Hippie Aesthetic” and are intended for listening, Hard Rock songs are often at a good tempo for
dancing; not too slow, not too fast. However, Heavy Metal songs are often at extremely fast or slow tempos
which make more of a show of the technique of the musicians (it’s harder to play really slowly or really
quickly).
Hard Rock often used basic musical structures such as Verse/Chorus and 12-Bar Blues, while Heavy Metal
would often use longer, more complex structures that often had a similarity to those of classical music.
3/ At the Hard Rock end of the scale there is a much more pronounced influence of blues. In part, this is due to
the origin of the style. As we sill see, many of the early bands emerged from the British Blues Revival, and
Psychedelic Blues is considered an important precursor. The blues influence may be heard in song structures,
approaches to guitar solos and riffs, and vocal style. But as we move to the Heavy Metal end of the scale, the
blues influences become harder to hear and there is more of a focus on complex technique and musicianship
(see point 2 above). One of the influences is western classical music. We hear this in more complex musical
structures, changes of tempo, highly coordinated ensemble playing and virtuosic guitar solos.

4/ Hard Rock lyrics often fall into the Chuck Berry traditions of girls, cars, no school, and rock and roll. Heavy
Metal lyrics often deal with elements of fantasy, religion, and darker psychological states such as paranoia and
insanity. Alienation and loss of control are also common themes in Metal. The futility and horror of war is often
used as a symbol of these feelings. Both are reflective of the shift from community focus to the focus on the
individual. In the first case, forgetting your problems and just having a good time. In the second case, a feeling
that the problems are overwhelming.

While there are many bands who helped to develop the sound of HR/HM, most historians tend to focus on three
British bands that appear in the late 1960s: Black Sabbath, Deep Purple and Led Zeppelin. Part of the reason
why the style first develops in Britain may be that British culture did not experience the counterculture in the
same way as the US. It spends much of the 1950s rebuilding after WWII and by the mid 1960s the British
economy was already falling into recession. There was no post-war prosperity experienced by British youth.
This may be why their bands turned away from the collective ideals of the counterculture before American
bands did.

Let’s have a brief look at each band and see how they use the elements of HR/HM. Remember as we go through
the examples that we are not trying to decide if each band is HR or HM. What we are doing is looking at how
all three have elements of both – but the elements will change from one band to the next.

Our first band is Black Sabbath. Singer Ozzie Osborn has discussed how British youth of the late 1960s did not
feel the same level of optimism that their north American counterparts were experiencing. He states that the
bands were trying to capture this sense of ‘doom’ that was felt by the British audience.

Listen to Black Sabbath’s ‘War Pigs’

Notice that the song has a high number of section and tempo changes. There are numerous instrumental sections
included a drum solo quite early in the song. The lyrics deal with a loss of control and the futility of war. All are
characteristics that are associates with Heavy Metal.
There is one way in which Black Sabbath does lean back towards the Hard Rock end of the scale. Osborn’s
voice does not have a high level of distortion (unlike many singers in this style). In fact, his voice is one of the
most recognizable in HR/HM because of Osborn’s clearer, often more nasal vocal tone.

Deep Purple:
           - also British
 “Highway Star” (1972 – from the album Machine Head) - “Berry-style” lyrics
           - virtuosic vocal performance during the introduction
           - use of electric organ (Jon Lord)
           - classical influences in musicianship and approach to solos

Another important early band was Deep Purple. In many ways, DP was like BS with its layout of vocal, guitar,
bass, and drums, but there was a key difference: keyboardist Jon Lord. Most rock guitarists tend to be self
taught, but keyboardists often have years of formal lessons which expose them to the works of classical
European composers. Because he had extensive training in classical music, Jon Lord could bring the classical
traditions of composition and technicality to the band’s work. He is one of the musicians who integrate classical
traditions into Hard Rock/Heavy Metal.

Listen to Deep Purple’s ‘Highway Star.’

Notice that in many ways this song seems closer to the Hard Rock end of the spectrum. The lyrics
(Nobody’s gonna take my car…Nobody’s gonna take my girl) are very much in the tradition of Chuck Berry.
And while the song does have several different sections, the tempo remains steady – not too slow, not too fast.
However, there is a high level of distortion including in the vocal performance. And there are ‘virtuosic’
elements of performance such as the vocals in the introduction. Also, listen to the keyboard solo starting at 2:14.
Lord plays a quick, almost mathematical series of notes. Anyone with experience in classical music will
recognize this precise, patterned approach as being influenced by classical music – in this case the music of the
European Baroque era (Bach, Vivaldi). This highly technical approach to soloing with become an important
element for all musicians in the style, particularly when we reach the HM end of the spectrum.

The final of our three bands is Led Zeppelin. They are by far the hardest to pin down simply because of the
range of things they did. The band had its origins in the British Blues Revival. Guitarist Jimmy Page had
recently joined a group call The Yardbirds. The Yardbirds were an important Blues Revival group that featured
guitarists such as Eric Clapton. Shortly after Page joined, the band decided to break up. Page, left without work,
continued on with a new temporary group named The New Yardbirds, simply to give himself time to find
something else long term. But the new group did so well that they decided to stay together and changed their
name to Led Zeppelin.

Because of their roots in the Blues Revival, Led Zeppelin always had a strong blues influence in their work. But
even early on, the group was experimenting with a more acoustic celtic/folk-influenced sound and Jimmy Page
(influenced by The Beatles) would become fascinated by the potential of the recording studio. Also in keeping
with the ‘Hippie Aesthetic’ the band had no interest in shortening their songs to fit the requirements of singles
for hit radio, even when refusing to do so could potentially cost the band significant income. First and foremost,
they were focused on albums.

Listen to Led Zeppelin’s “Whole Lotta’ Love.”
This song is an excellent example of both the influence of blues and the growing fascination with creative use
of the recording studio.
The song was actually a reworked version of a Muddy Waters’ song called “You Need Love” composed by
Chess Records’ Willie Dixon. Indeed, the LZ version is so close to the original that Dixon successfully sued the
group for royalties and writing credit in the early 1980s.
The song is riff-based, and Robert Plant’s vocal approach is similar to Muddy Waters. Plant is just singing in a
higher range. Given that the song is a reworked version of Willie Dixon’s song, the lyrics are clearly in the
tradition of blues.
But at 1:18, the nature of the song changes radically. Rather than a straight forward, blues-influences sound, the
recording becomes experimental – almost abstract. This is a good example of the overlap between HR and HM
and the music of the counter culture. This middle section can be described as ‘psychedelic.’ Now, some of you
may get grumpy thing: ‘Hey didn’t you say that counter culture was separate from NR/HM?” In general yes,
but like most points of cultural change, the later style is always influenced by the earlier style even if the later
style ends up rejecting many of the earlier style’s values.

The style may be viewed as a response to the failure of counter culture and a rejection of the collective ideal.
With the collapse of the counter culture and a growing sense cynicism due to the major events of the 1970s,
Hard Rock/Heavy Metal became an expression of the dissatisfaction that many young people had with the state
of society. The Hard Rock end of the spectrum reflected a desire to ignore the problems and simply focus on
pleasure, while Heavy Metal was an outwards reflection of youths’ fears and pessimism. However, the style
also offered a solution to the problem. Rather than focus on the collective, this is a style that focuses on the
greatness of the individual: musical greatness, fame and financial success. To this end the bands create images
that have a great deal in common with corporations. Most corporations have singular names (‘Sony’ or ‘Apple’
not ‘The Sonys’ or ‘The Apples’) The names of bands in HR/HM are often singular (Black Sabbath, Led
Zeppelin, AC/DC, Kiss, Aerosmith) as opposed to plural, collective names of the 1960s (The Beatles, The
Beach Boys, The Rolling Stones). They made use of (and continue to make use of) logos, similar to
corporations, that remain consistent throughout their careers.

They became a model of success for their fans who often imagine themselves as part of the band.

The Origins of Hip Hop

Hip Hop:
  - folk culture
  - South Bronx, New York City – 1970s
  - Rap / Break Dancing / Graffiti Art

 By the early 1970s, predominantly black inner-city neighborhoods were suffering. Following the civil
unrest of the mid to late 1960s, many within black urban communities who had achieved a middle-class
standard of living had moved
                                                                     to the suburbs leaving only working-
class families, and the poor. This had a serious impact on local economy and standard of living for those
who remained. Such was the case in the Bronx neighborhood of New York City. To make things worse,
in 1972, the Cross Bronx Expressway was completed, meaning the whole neighborhood could now be
avoided except for those who lived there. Local standards of living collapsed.
However, the young people of the area still wished to find ways of artistic expression. Like Great
Britain after World War II, the youth of the Bronx adopted a folk-like, Do-ItYourself (DIY) approach
to creating culture. In the world of dance we see the development of Break Dancing. In visual art, the
technique of Graffiti Art takes shape. And while music lessons and instruments were too expensive for
most, there was access to other resources and a technique of creating new music using records,
turntables and the human voice began to take shape. This was the origin of Rap.

Precursors:

   Example: “Signifyin’ Monkey”

While we have spent much of the term focusing on the importance of sound and articulation within African
American music, the use of words as an aesthetic/expressive medium is also an important characteristic with its
origins in West-African culture.
One example of this is “Signifyin’” or “Playing The Dozens” which involves both a type of poetic story-telling
as well as a form of ritualized contest of insulting your opponent.
Note that the performance, recorded in the early 1960s, lacks any type of rhythmic accompaniment, but many of
the elements of Hip Hop are here: speed, improvisation, rhyming, and creativity are highly valued.

2/ Jamaican Toasting
                                         - yard dances / Sound system men/DeeJay
                                         - SSM become 1st Jamaican recording producers
                                         - Producing for Yard Dances
                                         - Same song on both sides, one without vocals - Toasting
                                         - SSM would vocally improvise over the “B” side.

                                         While there are several important precursors to the development of
Hip Hop, perhaps the most important was a style of playing records at dances that was imported from Jamaica.
Jamaica has long been one of the poorest regions in the western hemisphere. Few owned a record player, and
while radios were more common, local radio stations were controlled by the BBC and rarely played anything
that could be considered “popular” music. However, radios could occasionally pick up American radio playing
R&B and Rock ‘n’ Roll. Both styles were popular with those who heard them.
Sound System men were Jamaican DJs who would stage large outdoor parties. Much of their popularity was
based on acquiring American music, and eventually they began to produce their own records for use at Yard
Parties. While one side of the record would feature the song, the other would feature a “remix” without most of
the vocals. The Sound System Men would put on the second side and begin to talk on the microphone, often
talking about how wonderful they were, and how much better their parties were as compared to their
competitors. This became known as Toasting.

                                     Although there is some debate, Grand Wizard Theodore is generally
                                    credited with the invention of “scratching,” a technique in which the DJ
                                    manually manipulates the record to create sound, rather than allowing the
record to play as intended. With this technique, Theodore completes the process of redefining the technology of
the turntable from one of consumption to production. As a technology of consumption, the turntable is used to
play a record as intended. Your role is simply to consume the recording. But in Theodore’s hands, the turntable
becomes a technology of production. It is used to create something new, like a musical instrument.

By 1979, this style had become very popular within the South Bronx, but was still largely unknown outside this
small part of New York City. Some local record producers had considered recording some rap, but it was not
until the end of that year that Sylvia Robinson, a minor soul star from the 1960s and owner of a local record
label called Sugar Hill Records, produced the song “Rapper’s Delight.” Featuring three local rappers, the lyrics
were rapped over music based on the most popular song of the year, a disco hit called “Good Times” by Chic

Over the first half of the 1980s, Rap grew in popularity with younger black audiences, but was largely
rejected by white fans of older styles such as Rock and even older black audiences who preferred soul and
funk. Nevertheless, it may have had a broader impact except that 18 months after the release of “Rapper’s
Delight’ the music industry experienced a huge change. MTV, the first 24-hour music-video station began
broadcasting in the summer of 1981. For the first time, fans had access to music videos, 24 hours a day. At
first, the music industry dismissed MTV, but by the end of 1981, it was obvious that “high-rotation” on MTV
was rapidly becoming the most influential source of hit records. By 1982, exposure on MTV was central to a
successful career. However, the founders of MTV were largely white and middle-class. The station had a clear
bias towards a white rock and pop audience but featured little coverage of early Rap videos in its
programming. In fact, all black artists found they had a harder time having videos put into rotation including
Michael Jackson. Given how important MTV had become in generating and promoting hit songs, this was a
significant problem.
But in 1985, Hip-Hop artists Run DMC collaborated with 1970s’ stadium-rock stars Aerosmith to produce a
Hip Hop version of the rock band’s 1977 hit, “Walk This Way.” The original 1977 recording featured a drum
intro that had long been popular among Hip Hop DJs.
