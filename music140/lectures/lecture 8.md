EXAMPLE: The Beatles “Tomorrow  Never Knows” from the album ‘Revolver’ (1966)
- Composed by John Lennon
- Lyrics are based on the Tibetan Book of the Dead  
	- Spent a lot of time studying spirituality in India
		- Also a good break from their popularity
- Song is based on a drone (use of the Tamboura - influence of non-western culture)
	- No chord changes, same group of notes throughout entire piece
	- Tamboura is hard to master, individual plucks need to sound continuous
	- Influence of Indian Classical Music
- Vocal is “double-tracked” and is run through a “Leslie” speaker cabinet
	- Exploring recording technology
	- Beatles don't invent double tracking but really embrace it
		- Singer records 1 vocal performance first, then records a second one that tries to perfectly match it
		- If it's really close, it sounds like a single voice with "something about it"
		- Johns voice is double tracked, if you pay attention you can tell
	- Parts of Johns voice is ran through the Leslie and rerecorded
		- Leslie is designed for organs
		- The speaker in the cabinet is on a turn table, the spinning adds a "shimmer" to the sound, bandwidth reduced
- makes use of tape “loops” (avant-garde, cutting edge experimentation) and backwards recording
	- Something Paul introduced
	- Tape Loops:
		- Record something onto a narrow looped strip of tape, run the loop through the tape machine over and over again
		- Can manipulate the loop, increase/decrease speed, reverse it, etc.
		- Origin of loops in modern music
		- Band creates 8 separate ones for this song (including the seagull sound, which was really Paul laughing), they run all 8, and adjust the volume each throughout the song
	- Backwards Recording
		- Hear it in the solos, two played by George
		- 1st is played on Citar (Indian instrument), 
		- 2nd is electric guitar
		- Both are backwards
1) Demonstrates the growing influence of non-western culture, and the technology of the recording studio on the work of The Beatles.
2) This song would have been unplayable in concert in the 1960s (would need to tour with 8 tape machines, and these are analog machines that aren't perfect, can't play guitar backwards) ... The band was losing interest in public performance.
	- The large loud crowds was fun at first but they got annoying
	- They want people to actually listen to them
	- They got tired of songs
