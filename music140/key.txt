KEY TERMS — COMMST 204 Week 7 Day 1 (Intro to Small Groups)

Organizational culture (Edgar Schein): “a pattern of shared basic assumptions that have been invented, discovered, or developed by a group as it learns to cope with problems of external adaptation and internal integration.”

Organizational culture is visible in: actions, stories, and artifacts that characterize an organization.

Organizational culture is also characterized by: values and assumptions.

Leaders can influence culture by shaping: values, attitudes, and “ways of doing things.”

Leaders must negotiate cultural realities: even if they don’t want to.

Two perspectives on culture:
- Deliberate creation of culture
- Organic, spontaneous emergence of culture

Groups and teams: a big part of how people experience organizations.

Formal groups/teams: more formal structures with rules, goals, and clear leadership structures.

Less formal groups/teams: norms, rules, and leaders emerge.

Leadership in groups/teams: may be assigned or may emerge.

Advantages of groups/teams in the workplace:
- Varied expertise
- Possible diversity
- Benefits of discussion and deliberation

Disadvantages of groups/teams:
- Can be inefficient
- Require ongoing, effective communication
- Not all members buy in
- People can coast on others’ efforts
- Require nurturing and care
- Require ongoing reflection by leaders and other members

Good groups/teams (goal): take advantage of individual skills and traits to achieve group goals (tough to do).

Learning group (Ronald Adler): “The purpose of a learning group is to increase the knowledge and skill of each member.”

Interpersonal communication in groups: individual satisfaction contributes to group and team satisfaction.

Democracy in teams (argument): individual satisfaction supporting group satisfaction is a good argument in favour of democracy in teams.

Authoritarianism (in teams): especially without coercive mechanisms, can lead to disengagement.

Self-concept (Beebe et al.): “Your subjective description of who you think you are—it is filtered through your own perceptions.”

Self-concept vs others’ perceptions: self-concept is not the same as how others perceive us.

Decenter / perspective-take (in groups/teams): remember to do this in group or team life.

Self-esteem (low): sensitive to affronts to one’s views; difficulty contributing; reluctance to take chances.

Self-esteem (high): generally a “thicker skin,” but possible unwillingness to incorporate others’ views.

Previous experiences (lens): perceptions of others are interpreted through the lens of previous experiences.

Perception (issues to recall):
- Confirmation bias
- Self-serving bias
- Attribution problems
- Negotiation: discussion of what the things we witness mean
- Factions

Task role / task-guiding behaviors (Fujishin): behaviors that initiate and maintain a productive task dimension during the group’s discussions.

Maintenance role / social-guiding behaviors (Fujishin): behaviors that encourage and maintain a healthy social dimension during group discussion.

Self-centered role (Eisenberg et al.): a team member who seeks to dominate discussions/work or divert attention from serious issues by making them seem unimportant; always considered inappropriate and unproductive.

Examples of team roles (Benne & Sheats terms as discussed in Fujishin):
- Task roles (positive): information requestor; information giver; information clarifier; discussion guider/summarizer; evidence analyzer/reasoning tester; negotiator; initiator/opinion giver
- Maintenance roles (positive): encourager; feeling expressor; harmonizer; energizer; social supporter; tension reliever
- Self-centered roles (negative): negative blocker (contrarian); dominator; attacker; clown; player; special-interest pleader

Demos (Gastil): “a useful shorthand term for a body of people who govern themselves democratically.”

Democracy (in small groups) vs Autocracy (authoritarian):
- Equality of voices vs Hierarchy of voices
- Respect for all views (shared wisdom) vs Little respect for views of less prominent members
- Persuasion rather than compulsion vs Rules/rewards/punishments from the top
- Decisions by consensus or vote vs Decisions by leader(s)
- Multidirectional communication vs Downward communication
- The group counts most vs The leader counts most
