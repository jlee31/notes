## Post-War Britain

- UK hit hard by WWII — rationing continued until **1952–53** (8 years post-war)
- No jobs, no factories → youth suffered most
- DIY culture emerged; American R&B records came in via port cities (Liverpool, London)
- **Skiffle** — white British teens playing American R&B with acoustic guitar, washtub bass, etc.
  - "Rock Island Line" — Lead Belly (1937) → Lonnie Donnegan skiffle version (**1955**)

## British Invasion — The Beatles

### Timeline

- **1957** — Lennon meets McCartney in Liverpool (The Quarrymen)**March 1958** — George Harrison joins
- **Summer 1960** — Pete Best on drums
- **August 1960** — "The Beatles" formed
- **December 1961** — Brian Epstein becomes manager (cleaned up image: suits, gel hair, skinny ties)
- **June 1962** — Signed to EMI/Parlophone; producer **George Martin** (classically trained)
- Ringo replaces Pete Best
- **September 1962** — "Love Me Do" (minor hit; Ringo on tambourine, studio drummer)
- **January 1963** — "Please Please Me" — breakthrough (AABA form)
- **November 1963** — Royal Variety Performance (Lennon's "rattle your jewellery" joke)
- **Feb 9, 1964** — Ed Sullivan Show, **70M US viewers** (out of 190M pop) → END of "in-between years"
- **Feb 10, 1964** — Washington Coliseum (first arena-style rock concert)
- **April 4, 1964** — Beatles hold **top 5** spots on Billboard Hot 100
- **July 1965** — film *Help*
- **August 15, 1965** — Shea Stadium, 55,000 people (invented modern stadium concerts)
- **March 1966** — Lennon "more popular than Jesus" comment
- **August 29, 1966** — Candlestick Park, SF — last public concert
- **June 1967** — *Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band* — first "concept album"
- **August 1967** — Brian Epstein dies (OD)
- **Fall 1969** — Band breaks up
- **Spring 1970** — McCartney officially announces departure

### Key Songs

- **"Please Please Me"** (Jan 1963) — AABA, hook-driven pop
- **"Yesterday"** (1965, from *Help*) — McCartney alone, string quartet, AABA, more serious lyrics; marks shift away from pop
- **"Tomorrow Never Knows"** (from *Revolver*, 1966) — Lennon; based on Tibetan Book of the Dead; tamboura drone, double-tracked vocals through Leslie speaker, tape loops, backwards recording; unplayable live
- **"A Day in the Life"** (*Sgt. Pepper's*, 1967) — two songs spliced; aleatory orchestra; final 3-piano chord sustained

### Beatles influences/evolution

- Idolized **Brian Wilson** and **Bob Dylan** (Dylan told them their lyrics said nothing)
- George Martin pushed classical elements

## Soul Music

**Fusion of:** Gospel vocals + R&B rhythm + TPA arrangements/lyrics

### Motown — Detroit ("Hitsville USA")

- Founded **1959** by **Berry Gordy** — modeled on auto assembly line
- Songwriters: Holland/Dozier/Holland, Smokey Robinson
- **Maxine Powell** — finishing school (how to present to white audience)
- **Cholly Atkins** — choreographer (tight, controlled movements)
- **The Funk Brothers** — house band; **James Jamerson** the anchor
- Absolute consistency of product; lip-syncing common

**Key tracks:**

- Smokey & the Miracles — "You'd Better Shop Around" (1960)
- The Supremes — **"Stop! In the Name of Love"** (H/D/H, **1965**) — vibraphone, less gospel (Diana Ross)

### Stax — Memphis ("Soulsville USA")

- Founded **1959** by Jim Stewart + Estelle Axton (St+Ax); Satellite Records → Stax in 1961
- House band: **Booker T & the M.G.s** — mixed race (unusual for the South)
  - "Green Onions" (**1962**) — #3 Pop, #1 R&B
- Monophonic, no multitrack; focus on energy over accuracy
- Less arrangement than Motown

**Key tracks:**

- **Otis Redding — "Try a Little Tenderness"** (1966) — reworked 1933 Bing Crosby TPA song; died in **1967** plane crash at 27
- Sam & Dave — "Soul Man" (1967)

### Atlantic / FAME / Muscle Shoals (Alabama)

- **Aretha Franklin — "Respect"** (1967) — originally Otis Redding; she flipped gender roles; became civil rights anthem

## Counterculture

### Roots

- **The Beats** — Kerouac, Ginsberg
- Beatitude — seeing the world as it really is; reject consumerism
- Revived early 1960s as "hippies"

### Main locations

1. **Greenwich Village** (NYC)
2. **Haight-Ashbury** (San Francisco)

### Characteristics

- Collective over individual; experience over material
- Sensory stimulation (psychedelic), drugs, poster art, tie-dye
- Music: loud, long song forms, jamming, lighting shows

### Key tracks / bands

- **"Truckin'"** — Grateful Dead (1970) — loose jamming, no set list, Deadheads
- **"White Rabbit"** — Jefferson Airplane (1967) — bolero rhythm, *Alice in Wonderland*, drug refs, dynamics-based structure
- **"Tomorrow Never Knows"** — Beatles (1966) — beginning of pop psychedelia
- **Jimi Hendrix** — "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)" (1967) — whammy bar, wah-wah, blues, heavy distortion

### 1967 — Summer of Love vs Long Hot Summer

- Counterculture became more politically active; anti-war protests
- **Yippies** (Youth International Party) — Jerry Rubin, Abbie Hoffman
- **Psychedelic Blues** emerges (Hendrix)

## Hard Rock / Heavy Metal

### Hippie Aesthetic shift (started with *Sgt. Pepper's*)

- Rock 'n' Roll → Rock
- Singles → albums; dancing → listening; entertainers → artists

### HR vs HM continuum


|            | Hard Rock                       | Heavy Metal                                  |
| ---------- | ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Distortion | Moderate                        | Heavy (guitar, bass, vocals)                 |
| Tempo      | Mid-tempo (danceable)           | Extreme fast or slow                         |
| Structure  | Verse/chorus, 12-bar blues      | Long, complex, classical-like                |
| Influence  | Blues                           | Classical (Bach, Vivaldi)                    |
| Lyrics     | Chuck Berry (girls, cars, rock) | Fantasy, religion, alienation, war, insanity |


### Spectrum examples

- **AC/DC** (Hard Rock) — Australian, formed **1973**; *Back in Black* (1980) — **2nd highest selling album in history, 50M+**
  - "Highway to Hell" — blues riff, steady tempo, Chuck Berry-style duck walk (Angus Young)
- **Metallica** (Heavy Metal) — LA, formed **1981**
  - "Master of Puppets" — loss of control, tempo changes, virtuosic solos, heavy distortion

### Founding Bands (British)

Why British first? No post-war prosperity; rejected collective earlier.

**1. Black Sabbath**

- Ozzy Osbourne — nasal, clear voice (less distortion than typical)
- "Doomy music," futility of war
- **"War Pigs"** (1970, *Paranoid*) — tempo changes, instrumental sections, alienation lyrics, lower distortion

**2. Deep Purple**

- **Jon Lord** — classically trained keyboardist, electric organ
- Brought classical (Bach/Vivaldi Baroque) to rock
- **"Highway Star"** (1972, *Machine Head*) — Chuck Berry-style lyrics, virtuosic vocal intro, keyboard solo at 2:14 (Baroque)

**3. Led Zeppelin**

- Grew out of **The Yardbirds** (Jimmy Page)
- Strong blues + acoustic/Celtic influence; studio mastery; no interest in singles
- **"Whole Lotta Love"** (1969, *Led Zeppelin II*) — blues riff; middle section psychedelic
- Successfully sued by **Willie Dixon** (from "You Need Love," Muddy Waters 1962)

### Band names

- 60s: plural/"The" (Beatles, Stones, Beach Boys)
- HR/HM: singular, corporate (Black Sabbath, Led Zeppelin, AC/DC, Kiss, Aerosmith) with logos

## Hip Hop

### Context

- South Bronx, NYC, early 1970s
- Black middle class moved to suburbs; **1972 Cross Bronx Expressway** — neighborhood collapses
- DIY folk culture with 3 elements: **Rap, Break Dancing, Graffiti Art**

### Precursors

**1. Signifying / The Dozens**

- African-origin oral word game; ritualized insults
- "Signifyin' Monkey"

**2. Jamaican Toasting**

- Sound System Men = Jamaican DJs at yard dances
- B-side = instrumental; SSM improvises vocally over it
- SSMs become first Jamaican record producers

### Key figures

- **1973 — Kool Herc** (Clive Campbell, Jamaican immigrant)
  - Two turntables; extends "breaks"
  - **"Merry-Go-Round"** technique; "cutting and mixing"
- **1976 — Grandmaster Flash**
  - Developed **"Quick Mix"** — backspinning for precise control
- **1976 — Grandmaster Melle Mel**
  - First to call himself **MC** (Master of Ceremonies)
  - First rapper to write down verses → first Hip Hop songwriter
- **1978 — Grand Wizard Theodore**
  - Invented **scratching** — turntable from consumption → production tech

### Key songs

- **"Rapper's Delight"** — Sugar Hill Gang (**Sept 1979**) — first recorded hit
  - #4 R&B, #36 Pop
  - Produced by **Sylvia Robinson** (Sugar Hill Records)
  - Based on Chic's **"Good Times"** (June 1979, #1 Pop/R&B)
- **"Walk This Way"** — Run DMC / Aerosmith (**1986**)
  - Original Aerosmith 1977; drum intro popular with DJs
  - Broke rap onto MTV

### MTV

- **August 1981** — MTV launches (24-hr music video)
- Biased toward white rock; rap excluded until **1985–86** "Walk This Way"
- By 1982, MTV rotation was essential for hits
