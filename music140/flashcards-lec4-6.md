## Disc Jockeys & Radio

**Q:** What is a **Disc Jockey (DJ)** and where does the term come from?
**A:** A DJ plays records on the radio to fill time between live content. "Jockey" as in riding/guiding a horse — originally an insult because live performance was preferred over recorded music.

**Q:** What is **Top 40 Radio** and where did the format come from?
**A:** A format where the 40 most-played jukebox songs are rotated repeatedly on radio. 40 was considered the optimal number for audience engagement. It standardized what got airplay and made repeat exposure the norm.

## Cover Versions (1954–1956)

**Q:** What is a **cover version** in the context of 1950s pop?
**A:** A "white" remake of a "black" (R&B) record aimed at the larger, more profitable pop (white) market. Covers typically smoothed out roughness, removed sexual references, and used more restrained production.

**Q:** What were **crossover hits**?
**A:** Songs that charted in multiple categories (Pop, R&B, Country & Western) simultaneously. Some artists would appear in the top five across different charts at once.

**Q:** What was the **moral panic** around R&B crossing over?
**A:** Some white parents objected to their children listening to "race music." The growing influence of Black culture triggered what the course calls "Oral Panic" in racist white communities.

**Q:** "Tutti Frutti" — original vs. cover: who, when, label, chart positions?
**A:** Original: Little Richard (Penniman/LaBostrie, 1955) on Specialty Records — #2 R&B, #17 Pop. Cover: Pat Boone (1956) — #10 Pop only. Pat Boone's version was smoother and safer but never charted R&B.

**Q:** What musical feature did Little Richard use in "Tutti Frutti"?
**A:** Stop-time (a pause where most instruments drop out), built on a modified 12-bar blues.

**Q:** "Sh-Boom" — original vs. cover differences in form and sound?
**A:** Original: The Chords (Cat Records, June 1954) — AABA + solo, rougher sound, close-mic crooning (you can hear breathing), saxophone, sexual metaphors — #3 R&B, #5 Pop. Cover: The Crew Cuts — AABA AABA (no solo), smoother, distant mic, control and restraint, **timpani** (classical instrument) instead of sax — #1 Pop for 9 weeks but brief.

**Q:** Why did race-based cover versions stop by 1956?
**A:** The covers were no longer making more money than the originals. The market had shifted and audiences increasingly preferred the authentic versions.

## Rock 'n' Roll Emerges

**Q:** Who is credited with coining the term **Rock 'n' Roll** and what was his role?
**A:** Alan Freed, a popular disc jockey on ABC radio. The phrase itself comes from African American culture and refers to having a party / good time (and carries sexual connotations).

**Q:** What does Rock 'n' Roll blend musically?
**A:** Pop, Country & Western, and R&B.

**Q:** Who was **Bill Haley** and why is his birth year important (test answer)?
**A:** Bill Haley (born **1925**) was the frontman of Bill Haley and the Comets (originally the Saddlemen). One of the first artists to emerge in the rock and roll style; began recording covers of R&B songs.

**Q:** "Rock Around the Clock" — who, writers, date recorded vs. date it became popular?
**A:** Bill Haley and the Comets; written by Freedman/Myers; recorded **1954**, became hugely popular in **1955** after appearing in the film *Blackboard Jungle*. Uses 12-bar blues, guitar/voice, up-tempo for fast dancing.

**Q:** Why was Bill Haley ultimately replaced by Elvis as the face of rock and roll?
**A:** Teenagers wanted to listen to other teenagers. Bill Haley was **30 years old** when "Rock Around the Clock" hit — too old to be a teen idol.

## Elvis Presley

**Q:** What are the key biographical facts about Elvis Presley?
**A:** Born 1935 in Tupelo, Mississippi (near Memphis / WDIA). His family attended both white and Black churches. Grew up listening to Dean Martin (TPA/pop), WDIA (race music), and church music. Died 1977 (age 42) of a drug overdose.

**Q:** Who was **Sam Phillips** and what did he famously say?
**A:** Owner of **Sun Records** in Memphis. He had early success with BB King and Johnny Cash. He said: *"If I could find a white man with the singing voice of a black man, I could make a million dollars"* — meaning someone who grew up singing African American music (cultural blend, not about physical race).

**Q:** What happened at Elvis's first Sun Records session (July 1954)?
**A:** Sam Phillips had Elvis sing C&W because he was white. During a break, Elvis spontaneously sang an R&B song — **"That's Alright Mama"** (written by Arthur Crudup; earliest version by Blind Lemon Jefferson). Sam recognized it immediately and they developed it into a record.

**Q:** What were the musicians on Elvis's early recordings?
**A:** Scotty Moore (guitar), Bill Black (bass), and notably **no drummer** at first. DJ Fontana (drums) joined later, from 1954–1955 onward.

**Q:** How does Elvis's 1954 version of **"Blue Moon of Kentucky"** differ from Bill Monroe's original?
**A:** Monroe's original is in **4/4** (standard). Elvis's version is in **3/4 (waltz time)** — unusual for pop but not country. Elvis also treats the lyrics as secondary to sound — sings a sad song as if happy, focusing on the feel. Features **slap bass** (percussive effect on bass strings) and **slap echo** (tape echo effect), distorted timbres.

**Q:** What is **Rockabilly**?
**A:** Elvis's early style — a blend of Rock and Roll with Hillbilly (C&W). Nickname: "Hillbilly Cat."

**Q:** What did Elvis record during his 18 months at Sun (1954–1955)?
**A:** 6 records (12 songs) — all covers. One side R&B, one side C&W. Became a regional star with some national country chart success.

**Q:** Why did Sam Phillips sell Elvis's contract?
**A:** Sam lacked the capital (~$10 million) to press enough records for national distribution. He sold Elvis to **Colonel Tom Parker** / **RCA** for **$35,000**.

**Q:** What did Colonel Tom Parker do for Elvis in 1956?
**A:** Put Elvis on national TV **11 times**, making him a household name across America.

**Q:** Chart performance of Elvis's major 1956 hits?
**A:** "Heartbreak Hotel" (April–May 1956) — **#1 Pop, #5 R&B**. "Hound Dog" and "Don't Be Cruel" (Aug–Dec 1956) — both simultaneously **#1 Pop / Country / R&B** (briefly at the same time). "Love Me Tender" also released in this period.

**Q:** What happened when Elvis performed "Hound Dog" on *Texaco Star Theatre* (June 1956)?
**A:** He went over time, confused the musicians, made a spectacle — generated **10,000 letters of outrage** claiming he was corrupting youth. Two weeks later on *The Steve Allen Show*, Parker made Elvis perform in a humiliating, restrained way to calm public reaction.

**Q:** What was the longer-term trajectory of Elvis's career direction?
**A:** Colonel Tom Parker steered Elvis toward mainstream pop. After 1957, Elvis stopped charting in R&B. He went to the army (March 1958 – March 1960), then became a movie star (1961–1968 — 7 years without performing live). Final phase: **Las Vegas Residency** from 1970. Died August 16, 1977 (age 42, drug overdose — amphetamine addiction dating to army service).

**Q:** What was the **"1968 Comeback Special"**?
**A:** A television variety show Colonel Tom Parker arranged to revive Elvis's relevance. Elvis's very first song was "That's Alright Mama" — a return to his roots.

## Chuck Berry

**Q:** Key biographical facts about Chuck Berry?
**A:** Born October 18, **1926** in St. Louis, Missouri. Middle-class background (father was a carpenter). Had a criminal past as a young man but turned around. Died 2017.

**Q:** What distinguished Chuck Berry from other guitarists of his era?
**A:** He was **always holding a guitar** — it was his primary identity, not just an accessory. He was the most self-conscious artist of the era: he thought carefully about every element of his music to appeal to his target audience.

**Q:** What did Chuck Berry observe about rich people's music preferences and how did it shape him?
**A:** He noticed that wealthy people listened to artists like Nat King Cole — clear enunciation, lots of lyrics, polished presentation. He modeled his own approach on that, while also incorporating Black blues elements.

**Q:** How did Chuck Berry stand out in competitions?
**A:** Everyone else was imitating Muddy Waters (rough, deep blues sound). Chuck played with a nasal, brighter tone — standing out got him noticed and he would win.

**Q:** What label did Chuck Berry sign with, and what deal did they offer?
**A:** **Chess Records**, July 1955. The Chess brothers told Chuck he needed to **write his own songs** to get all the royalties — unlike Elvis who just sang other people's material.

**Q:** "Maybellene" — origins and chart performance?
**A:** Chuck Berry rewrote **"Ida Red"** by Bob Wills and the Texas Playboys (1938). Renamed using the name from a makeup bag. Released on Chess Records 1955. **#1 R&B, #5 Pop**.

**Q:** What are the three main **themes of Rock and Roll** according to the course?
**A:** **Cars** (freedom), **Girls** (women as objects to be pursued/acquired by men), **No School** (skipping class, rebellion). Chuck Berry's songwriting typified all three.

**Q:** Why did Chuck Berry lean into using the words "Rock and Roll" in his songs?
**A:** He recognized rock and roll as a symbol of youth culture — something young people had for themselves, distinct from their parents' TPA/pop world. Using the phrase spoke directly to his target audience: white teenagers.

**Q:** "Johnny B. Goode" (1958) — chart positions, fun fact, and musical elements?
**A:** #2 R&B, #8 Pop. Fun fact: the song is engraved on the **Voyager 1 gold record** launched into deep space. Starts with a guitar solo. Lyrics: text-heavy, narrative storytelling, clearly enunciated, talks about skipping school. Built on 12-bar blues.

**Q:** What WHITE elements and BLACK elements appear in "Johnny B. Goode"?
**A:** **White:** text-heavy narrative lyrics, clear enunciation, country guitar sound. **Black:** blues chord structure, added 6th (boogie-woogie — alternating between 1st and 5th notes with a driving left hand), guitar solo focused on timbre/articulation/rhythm.

**Q:** What is **boogie-woogie** as used in Chuck Berry's style?
**A:** A piano/guitar technique featuring a driving alternating pattern between the 1st and 5th notes of a scale, with the left hand (on piano) playing energetically while the right handles chords and solos.

**Q:** What is Chuck Berry's own description of how he targeted different audiences?
**A:** *"Listening to Nat Cole prompted me to sing sentimental songs with distinct diction. The songs of Muddy Waters impelled me to deliver the down-home blues in the language they came from. When I played hillbilly songs, I stressed my diction so that it was harder and whiter. All in all, it was my intention to hold both the black and the white clientele."*

**Q:** Why did the Beatles base themselves on Chuck Berry rather than Elvis?
**A:** Chuck Berry wrote all his own songs, always played guitar, and performed as a self-contained artist — a model for bands of the 1960s. Elvis only sang others' material and fit the old division-of-labour system.

## The Golden Age of Rock and Roll (1954–1959)

**Q:** What three metrics demonstrate rock and roll's growth during the Golden Age?
**A:** 1) **Revenue**: Industry worth $200M/year in 1954 → $600M/year by end of the era. 2) **Pop Top 10 share**: 15% R&R in 1955 → 42% by end of era. 3) **Independent label market share**: 21% in 1955 → 66% by end of era.

**Q:** Why did independent labels dominate rock and roll while major labels missed out?
**A:** Major labels stayed away from R&R. Only Elvis (via RCA) was on a major. Chuck Berry, Little Richard, etc. were on independents. The **division of labour** system at majors couldn't accommodate self-contained artists who wrote, played, and sang.

**Q:** What was the **Payola** scandal and how was it weaponized?
**A:** Payola = bribes to radio DJs to play certain records. It predated rock and roll (going back to 1920s). Major labels used the scandal to attack rock and roll, implicitly linking it with corruption. **Alan Freed** was the biggest target — lost his job, wife, went bankrupt, died in the mid-1960s.

**Q:** How did major labels use **racism** to attack rock and roll?
**A:** They implicitly linked race with inferior musical quality, claiming R&R was low-quality music. The argument was also tied to white fears about the growing **Civil Rights Movement**.

## Rock and Roll & the Civil Rights Movement

**Q:** What is the Civil Rights connection to rock and roll's opposition?
**A:** The rise of rock and roll coincided with the Civil Rights Movement. White middle-class dislike of R&R was linked to fear of racial integration. Both were seen as threats to the existing social order.

**Q:** What happened in **May 1954** (one month before Elvis went to Sun Records)?
**A:** **Brown v. Board of Education** — Supreme Court ruled that segregated schools violated the US Constitution.

**Q:** What happened in **December 1955**?
**A:** **Rosa Parks** refused to give up her bus seat in Montgomery, Alabama. She was arrested, triggering the **Montgomery Bus Boycott** — a mass refusal by Black residents to use the bus system.

**Q:** Who was **Martin Luther King Jr.** in this context?
**A:** The leader of the Civil Rights Movement; also the pastor at Rosa Parks's church. Advocated non-violent protest. Slowly, unjust laws were changed.

**Q:** What were **Freedom Riders** and **Lunch Counter Sit-ins**?
**A:** Tactics of non-violent civil rights protest. Freedom Riders challenged segregation on interstate buses. Sit-in protesters occupied "whites-only" lunch counters, returning daily until served or removed.

## The Great Extinction (1957–1959)

**Q:** What is the **Great Extinction** and what happened to each major figure?
**A:** The period in 1957–1959 when the leading rock and roll stars were taken out of circulation: • **Elvis** — drafted into the army March 1958. • **Chuck Berry** — arrested December 1959 under the Mann Act (transporting a woman across state lines for prostitution). • **Jerry Lee Lewis** — May 1958, his marriage to his 13-year-old cousin became public; dropped by Sun Records. • **Little Richard** — October 1957, became a born-again Christian priest after a plane engine exploded mid-flight (he prayed and left rock and roll). • **Buddy Holly** — died in a plane crash February 3, 1959, along with Ritchie Valens and "The Big Bopper" (JP Richardson).

**Q:** What is the **Mann Act** and why was Chuck Berry arrested?
**A:** A law making it illegal to transport women across state lines for purposes of prostitution. Chuck Berry transported a young woman from one side of the Mississippi River (a state line) to work as a hostess at his nightclub.

**Q:** What song is associated with Jerry Lee Lewis, and what was his background?
**A:** **"Great Balls of Fire"** (a song about literal great balls of fire). Lewis came from the poor rural South, was a pianist, and was known as "a rough diamond." Sun Records dropped him after his marriage scandal.

**Q:** What song is associated with Buddy Holly, and what pop cultural reference marks his death?
**A:** **"Peggy Sue"** (1956). His death (with Valens and Richardson) is referred to in Don McLean's song as **"the day the music died."**

## The In-Between Years (1959–1963)

**Q:** What were the "In-Between Years" bounded by?
**A:** The end of the Golden Age of Rock and Roll (early 1959) and the arrival of the Beatles (February 1964).

**Q:** How did major labels respond once the major R&R stars were gone?
**A:** They realized rock and roll was not going away. They looked for sub-genres that appealed to teenagers but still used the **division of labour** system — meaning professional songwriters, studio musicians, choreographed performers.

**Q:** What was the **Dance Craze** of the early 1960s?
**A:** A wave of dance-themed pop songs targeting teenagers. Examples: "The Twist" (Chubby Checker, written by Hank Ballard) and "The Locomotion" (Little Eva, written by Goffin/Carole King, 1962). Featured lots of background singing and professional studio bands.

**Q:** Who was **Little Eva** and what is notable about "The Locomotion"?
**A:** Eva Boyd, who was **Carole King's babysitter**. "The Locomotion" (1962, written by Gerry Goffin and Carole King) featured extensive background vocals — considered a pioneering use of background singing.

**Q:** What were **Teen Idols** and what made them appealing to the major label system?
**A:** Clean-cut, non-threatening, idealized boyfriend figures marketed to teenage girls (and their parents). They sang romantic, non-sexualized love songs and dance songs. Example: **Bobby Vinton** with "Blue Velvet" (1963).

**Q:** "Blue Velvet" — what is its actual origin and why is it notable?
**A:** Composed in **1950**; it was already a hit for Tony Bennett in 1951. It's a **TPA song**, not new rock and roll at all. Bobby Vinton's 1963 recording brought teenagers back to the TPA sound. The arrangement opens with an electric guitar — a nod to the new era even with old-fashioned content.

## The Brill Building

**Q:** What was the **Brill Building** and what was its function?
**A:** A few-block area in NYC where multiple floors were dedicated to music. It was the **new Tin Pan Alley** for the early 1960s — an industrialized songwriting factory for the teen pop market.

**Q:** What was **Aldon Music** and who ran it?
**A:** A publishing/songwriting company inside the Brill Building run by **Don Kirshner** and **Al Nevins**. Employed elite songwriting teams including Gerry Goffin & Carole King, and Neil Diamond (who wrote "Sweet Caroline").

**Q:** What song came out of the Brill Building and exemplifies the era?
**A:** **"You've Lost That Lovin' Feeling"** — performed by the Righteous Brothers, written by Brill Building writers.

**Q:** How is the Brill Building similar to TPA?
**A:** Both used division of labour (teams of specialized songwriters), both produced polished, commercial music for a mainstream audience, both were concentrated in NYC, and both prioritized accessible, broad-appeal content.

## Technology: Tape, Multitrack Recording, and the Producer

**Q:** Who was **Jack Mullin** and what did he discover?
**A:** A Sergeant in the US Marine Corps of Engineers who discovered the **Magnetophone** — a Nazi German invention that was the first high-quality magnetic tape-to-tape recording machine.

**Q:** What did **Bing Crosby** do with the Magnetophone?
**A:** Used it to pre-record his weekly radio broadcasts (rather than performing live). This was revolutionary — tape allowed **editing**: cutting out mistakes, splicing takes together. Jack Mullin became Crosby's assistant. Crosby invested in tape technology and helped create **AMPEX**, one of the biggest tape manufacturers in the world. In 1948, Crosby gave one of the first reel-to-reel recorders to **Les Paul**.

**Q:** Who was **Les Paul** (1915–2009) and what were his two major contributions?
**A:** 1) Invented the **solid-body electric guitar** ("The Log" — a guitar built from solid wood, later commercialized as the **Gibson Les Paul**). Solved the problem of acoustic guitars' quick sound decay. 2) Pioneered **multitrack recording** — splitting tape into independent audio streams so multiple instruments/vocals could be recorded and layered separately.

**Q:** What is **multitrack recording** and how did it change music production?
**A:** Recording multiple independent audio streams (tracks) that can be combined, edited, and adjusted separately. Les Paul worked with 4–8 tracks; by the 1970s, 16-track recording was common. It transformed recording from **reproduction** (capturing a live moment) to **production** (constructing a performance piece by piece over time).

**Q:** "Sitting on Top of the World" (Les Paul with Mary Ford, 1953) — what does it demonstrate?
**A:** Full use of multitrack recording: Les Paul played all the instruments; Mary Ford sang all the vocals (layered). Also demonstrates **varied speed recording** — recording at different tape speeds to create sounds humans physically can't make.

**Q:** What is the role of the **producer** and how did it evolve?
**A:** Originally: find musicians and songwriters, have them practice, get them in front of a microphone, hit record. In the in-between years, the producer became a creative force — shaping sound, arranging layers of instrumentation, and becoming as important as the performers.

**Q:** Who was **Phil Spector** and what was his signature technique?
**A:** One of the most influential producers of the early 1960s. Invented the **"Wall of Sound"** — layering many instruments together so densely that no single instrument is distinctly audible ("a musical stew"). He would record all instruments first, then add vocals on top.

**Q:** "Be My Baby" (The Ronettes, 1963) — who wrote it and what musical form does it use?
**A:** Written by Barry/Greenwich/Spector. Produced by Phil Spector (Wall of Sound). Uses **verse/chorus** form.

**Q:** What is **verse/chorus** form and how does it differ from AABA?
**A:** **Verse/Chorus:** Verse = same music, different words (tells a story). Chorus = same music AND same words (often includes the song title), with an increase in intensity/energy. In AABA, the song title typically appears in the first line. Verse/chorus form had existed before but became dominant in this era and beyond.

**Q:** What are **Girl Groups** and **Boy Bands** as a phenomenon?
**A:** Ensembles where everyone is a vocalist and choreographer (no instrumentalists). They become popular in cycles. The Ronettes are an early example of the girl group format.

## Song Form Summary

**Q:** List the three main song forms covered in the course so far and a key example of each.
**A:** 1) **AABA** — "Somewhere Over the Rainbow" (Judy Garland, 1939). 2) **12-bar blues** — "Rock Around the Clock" (Bill Haley, 1954); "Johnny B. Goode" (Chuck Berry, 1958). 3) **Verse/Chorus** — "Be My Baby" (The Ronettes, 1963).
