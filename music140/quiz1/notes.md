### North American Popular Music – 1945
Music scene was much like today with a few big artists and genres and old ones being phased out. 

Big bands: Perdedo:
- 15-20 musicians
- 4 distinct sessions
    - Front: 4-5 saxophones
    - Middle: 3 - 4 Trombones
    - Back: 4-5 Trumpet
    - Side: Rhythm Section
        - Drummer
        - Bass Player
            - Double bass
        - Guitar
        - Piano
    - Vocalist
        - Were not the stars, just part of the band
        - Much of the music was instrumental, music since has become more focused on the vocalist
- The music was intended for dancing
    - Notice the feel and groove, the rhythm is conducive for dancing
- Although it was phased out in the 40s, this type of music still lives on through things like jazz ensembles in school
    - Nothing ever goes away completely
- Declined for many reasons, one being that they are big and it's hard to keep many people together
- Bangs transitioned to be smaller and the vocalists become the stars (still this way today)


Example: Big Band - "Sentimental Journey” (Brown/Homer/Green, 1944):
- Names in the brackets are the composers/song writers
    - Contrast this with today where we barely know who writes most pop songs 
- Vocalist was Doris Day (this was the beginning of her career, she goes on to be very successful)
- Date was when it was published or initially recorded
- Most popular song of 1945
- Notice that Doris was not the focus of the music, Les Brown was the star
- Doris' vocals:
    - Smoothness, no distortion or roughness
    - Round and rich, clear articulation
    - Called crooning, crooners
    - Only made possible by microphones, as previous singers had to be loud to be heard over the band (shouters)
    - Lot of control and restraint
    - Chest resonance

The arc of popularity: 
- Happens to every style and artist

Categories:
- Pop: 
    - White middle class and urban for large audiences, hard to get this many people elsewhere
- Race: 
- Hillbilly: More on geography than race

- The categories are defined by their intended audience as much as what they sound like (especially for race and hillbilly)
    - Notice that there is no stipulation about the race of the artist despite there being one with the audience
- The difference between north and south reflects how the prejudice against northern and southerners that stemmed from the civil war. 
- These categories are essentially still with us today with different names
    - Hillbilly -> country
    - Race -> r&b -> ...

Example: Race Music - “Good Rockin’ Tonight” Performed by Wynonie Harris (Roy Brown,  
1947):
- Originally written and recorded by Roy Brown in 1947
- Sax solo
    - Still prevalent today, change in texture, vary the energy of the performance
- Sub style called jump blues
- Voice is more rough
    - More power (shouter)
    - Crunching his vocal chords
    - Chest resonance
- Instruments:
    - Smaller band, but still based on big band instrumentation
    - 2 sax, trumpet, instead of the full band

Example: Hillbilly - “Blue Moon of Kentucky” (Bill Monroe, 1946 – Recording released  
in 1947):
- Instruments:
    - More likely to find in a rural environment: Fiddle, Guitar, Mandolin
- Vocals:
    - Special way of resonating his voice, in his sinuses
    - Very little emotional response, emotional distance
        - Sing but don't overly emote

Parlour songs:
- For the middle class women that came about because of the industrial revolution

People could make music in this era but would not become extremely wealthy

EXAMPLE: Late Parlour Song: “After The Ball” (Charles K Harris, 1891)

## North American Slave Trade

## Work Songs
AUDIO EXAMPLE: “Old Alabama” 1947
- Given this name but technically never had one
- The songs were improvised
- Can be thought of as a game:
    - Song leader role was very valuable, carried around many songs "Floating pool of verses"
    - The individual were very valuable slaves as they increase productivity
    - Each "component" had two lines, the leader would sing the first line, the rest of the group jumps in when they figure out what he's singing
    - The leader either chooses to sing the first line or move on, and the group needs to jump in when they figure it out
    - The leader then chooses a new set of two lines
- Song leader would look out for weaker or sick members of the group, setting the pace so everyone seems to be pulling their weight
- Work songs are a type of fold music

### African Retentions

- Distortion is though of bad and unintended in most settings, but not in West African Music

- Call and Response:
    - Information/Energy flow, back and forth between audience and performer

## The Blues
- Appears in the 1890s, but no one bothers to record it until the 1920s (recording technology existed but the owners were focused on white music)
- Country (Rural) Blues:
    - Not to be confused with Hillbilly
    - Lone wandering and the danger associated contributed to the male dominance
    - Acoustic guitar was light and suited for travelling
    - Early blues had a more nasal vocal sound before it transitioned
    - Putting voice to their troubless
- Musical Form:
    - "Floating Pool of verse":
        - Artists would sometimes arrive at a town, learn about whats going on, and incorporate these events into their verses

AUDIO EXAMPLE: “Travelin’ Blues” Blind Willie McTell (1929)
- Early example, more "Folk" music still
- Subtle variation in the chord progression, very common

Robert Johnson: 
- As blues became more popular, artists become more self conscious as they cared more about finding an audience
- Guitar Hero: More nuanced and innovative ways to play the guitar
- Mythic Character:

## Changes in Technology

- Disks were much more durable, having two sides gave much more room for recording
- The technology transition into normal life was slow like most new technologies

- 78 rpm:
    - Compromise between storage and quality
    - Allowed more records to be sold as they could be used interchangeably 
### What was recorded?
- Early TPA members say records as a way to advertise sheet music 
    - Playing songs in stores
    - One reason why it wasn't seen as worth recording race and hillbilly music, there would be no sales to people in the countryside
- Cultural chauvinism was the main reason only pop music was recorded, poor and black were looked down on 
### Commercial Radio
- Everything that would've ended up on T.V was broadcast-ed through radio (cooking shows, children shows, etc.)
- In the early days everything was live including musical performances
- Local networking still existed in the large networks, during off hours
- Sheet music would take a long time to spread and become popular, the radio spread music much more quickly

- The success of radio almost ruined the record industry, very serious trouble by 1922
    - People got tired of records fast, not like today where people get very attached, so the idea of unlimited content was very appealing
### Rise of the Record Industry
- Perry Bradford and Ralph Peer
    - Need to expand the music of records beyond TPA to find new audiences
    - When Perry brought in Mamie Smith, people were surprised as she was black 
        - Given the audience of TPA, vocalists were always white
        - One of the first times a black vocalist sang in a pop song and was recorded in a non mocking way
    
Example: Mamie Smith: “Crazy Blues” (Perry Bradford 1920)
- Song is still TPA despite the vocalist and song writer
- Typical big band instruments (15 years prior to the big bang era), not race music
- Can also tell by her voice
    - Loud voice, no microphones, need to be heard over the band
    - Opposite from blues who sing in rural small settings
- Song became a hit, showed record companies:
    - 1) People were perfectly happy buying records performed by black vocalists
    - 2) There is a significant black market (they underestimated the size and amount of disposable income)

Crazy Blues success, and the declining middle class white audience lost to radio, encouraged record companies to record race and hillbilly music for their audiences. 
Ralph Peer was the one who coined the terms race and hillbilly.

EXAMPLE: Carr and Blackwell “How Long Blues” (1928)
