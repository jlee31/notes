# Music 140 – Master Answer Key (Lectures 1–7 + Tech & Rock ’n’ Roll Era)

A1. **Timbre** is the quality or “colour” of a sound – what makes two sounds with the same pitch and loudness (e.g., sax vs. violin) still sound different. It is crucial because many style differences in popular music (smooth vs. rough, distorted vs. clean) are timbral rather than purely melodic or harmonic.

A2. A typical **big band** in the 1930s–1940s had about **15–20 musicians** arranged in sections: 4–5 saxophones at the front, 3–4 trombones in the middle, 4–5 trumpets at the back, plus a **rhythm section** (drums, double bass, guitar, piano) and a vocalist. Its primary purpose was **dance music** – creating a strong groove for social dancing.

A3. In big bands, the **vocalist** was just one part of the ensemble, not the main star. Much of the music was instrumental, and the bandleader (e.g., Les Brown) was the focus. In later popular music (post‑war pop, rock, etc.), the **vocalist becomes the star** and central selling point.

A4. **Crooning** is a smooth, intimate singing style with clear articulation and controlled dynamics (e.g., Doris Day, Nat King Cole). It was only possible with **microphones** and amplification, which allowed singers to be heard over a band without shouting.

A5. **Pop**: intended audience = **white, middle‑class, urban, largely northern US**.  
**Race**: intended audience = **Black listeners**, regardless of class or region.  
**Hillbilly**: intended audience = primarily **white, working‑class, rural/southern US** (later rebranded as Country & Western).

A6. Because these categories were defined by **who the music was marketed to**, not by who performed it. For example, a Black singer like Nat King Cole could perform **Pop**, and Hillbilly was about rural white audiences even when it used instruments or styles with African roots (e.g., banjo).

A7. By **1891** there were more urban populations, more **disposable income**, better **distribution** (department stores), and faster communication (word of mouth in cities). This made it possible for sheet music like **“After the Ball”** to sell over **5,000,000 copies**, making its composer very rich and creating the first modern “hit.”

A8. A **Victorian parlour song** was sheet music intended for **middle‑class women** to sing and play at home (often on piano). It expressed Victorian values of **appearance, control, restraint, loyalty, and appropriate behaviour** – similar values later heard in smooth pop vocals like Doris Day.

A9. The **Industrial Revolution** created a wealthier middle class with **luxury items** like pianos and time for **leisure** (especially for daughters). This produced a market eager to purchase **sheet music** as a consumer product, leading to copyright changes and an organized music industry.

A10. **Tin Pan Alley (TPA)** was a cluster of publishers and songwriters in **Manhattan, NYC**, active especially in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Its **division of labour** meant different specialists wrote lyrics, composed melodies, arranged music, etc., to standardize production and maximize efficiency and quality.

A11. TPA lyrics tended to be **simple**, focusing on **idealized romance** (beginnings and endings of relationships) and avoiding controversial topics. This simplicity matched the needs and abilities of the **middle‑class amateur audience** who bought sheet music and wanted songs that were easy to sing and play.

A12. **AABA** is a 32‑bar song form where three similar sections (A A A) are separated by a contrasting **B** section (the bridge). The B section usually moves to new melodic material and often raises intensity or harmonic tension so that when A returns, it feels like a satisfying “homecoming.”

A13. A **floating pool of verse** is a shared stock of short lyric units (couplets) that blues or work‑song performers could recombine and adapt to new situations. Performers would learn many verses and then choose or improvise them on the spot, commenting on local events or personal experiences.

A14. **Country (rural) blues** typically featured a **solo singer with acoustic guitar**, used **12‑bar phrases**, an **a‑a‑b lyric pattern**, and **call‑and‑response** between voice and guitar. It was often improvised and closely tied to themes like travel and economics.

A15. **Robert Johnson** shows increased **self‑consciousness**: his intros and outros are carefully shaped, with dynamic control, silences, and complex guitar parts. Compared to earlier, more purely “folk” performers, he clearly thought deeply about his sound and how to impress listeners and potential audiences.

A16. **Work songs** coordinated labour, set the **pace**, and helped pass time. The **song leader** chose and improvised lines from a floating pool of verses; the group joined in when they recognized the line. The leader also watched the group’s physical condition and adjusted tempo so weaker workers could keep up.

A17. Key **African retentions** include:  
– **Percussion**: emphasis on drums, sharp attacks, and rhythmic drive.  
– **Distortion**: rough, “overloaded” timbres seen as expressive rather than mistakes.  
– **Riff**: a short, repeated musical figure forming the basis of a piece (modern “beats”).  
– **Call‑and‑response**: energy and information flowing back and forth between performer and audience, who are equally important.

A18. The move from **fragile cylinders** to **durable discs** (standardized at **78 rpm** by the 1910s) made records more practical for mass production, easier to store and ship, and allowed **two sides** of music. This solidified discs as the main recording medium.

A19. Early record companies were run by people already invested in **TPA and sheet music**, targeting middle‑class white audiences. They saw records primarily as **advertising for sheet music** and believed there was **no market** for Race or Hillbilly music, partly due to **class and racial prejudice**.

A20. **Mamie Smith’s “Crazy Blues”** proved two things:  
1) White consumers were **willing to buy records by Black vocalists**.  
2) There was a **significant Black market** with disposable income.  
Its success convinced record companies to record **Race and Hillbilly** music, expanding the industry.

A21. **Radio** delivered free, constantly changing content, including live music, into homes. Compared to relatively expensive records that repeated the same songs, radio felt more attractive. By **1922** the record industry was in serious trouble because people preferred listening to free radio instead of buying discs.

A22. **WDIA** (Memphis, 1948) started as a station playing pop records but got no advertisers. After discovering its strongest signal in **Black neighbourhoods**, it shifted to **Race records**. It became a crucial **black appeal station**, spreading R&B and signalling that certain businesses welcomed Black customers. Elvis, as a boy, listened to WDIA and absorbed its music.

A23. After WWII, North America had intact **infrastructure** and booming **factories** shifting from war to consumer goods, creating **post‑war prosperity** and the nuclear family. With many soldiers traumatized, people wanted **“safe” music** like Nat King Cole’s “Too Young” and Patti Page’s “Doggie in the Window” – smooth, restrained, non‑threatening pop.

A24. Economic prosperity allowed many middle‑class youths to **stay in school** and work part‑time rather than full‑time. This created the distinct social category of the **“teenager”** with its own money and tastes, leading industries (clothes, movies, music) to target them specifically with new products and styles.

A25. **Jump Blues** emerged as a fusion of **Big Band/Jazz** elements with stronger blues and Race‑music influences. Bands were **smaller** (sax, trumpet, rhythm section), faster, and focused more on the **vocal performer** and sax solos. The style supported energetic dancing and more intense timbres.

A26. **Ray Charles** fused the **energy and call‑and‑response of gospel** with **secular lyrics** (e.g., “I’ve Got a Woman” based on “It Must Be Jesus”). Signed to **Atlantic Records**, a major label, he gained **major promotion** and success with white audiences, showing that intense, gospel‑influenced R&B could cross over.

A27. **Chicago electric blues** (e.g., Muddy Waters’ “Hoochie Coochie Man”) amplified guitar and harmonica using **amps**, sometimes pushed into **distortion**. It often used **modified 12‑bar blues**, **stop‑time** effects, and a louder, more urban sound adapted to noisy city bars.

A28. A **disc jockey** is a radio host who **fills time by playing records** between live segments. The term was originally an **insult** (“jockeying” records rather than presenting live music), because live performance was seen as superior. DJs solved scheduling and content‑supply problems for stations.

A29. **Top 40 radio** took the **40 most popular jukebox songs** (based on play counts) and rotated them on air. Forty was considered an **optimal number** to keep audiences engaged without feeling too repetitive, and it standardized playlists and exposure.

A30. In the 1954–1956 context, **cover versions** were **white remakes of Black R&B records** aimed at the more profitable white Pop market. The pattern was controversial because it smoothed out Black musical features and **profited from racist market structures**, often overshadowing the original artists.

A31. In **“Tutti Frutti”**, Little Richard’s original used **modified 12‑bar blues**, **stop‑time**, rough timbres, and sexually suggestive lyrics; Pat Boone’s cover smoothed the vocal timbre, removed roughness and sexuality, and stayed squarely in a safe Pop style. For **“Sh‑Boom”**, The Chords’ version had doo‑wop harmonies, a sax solo, audible breathing, and an AABA + solo structure; The Crew Cuts’ cover cleaned the sound, removed intimacy and sax in favour of **timpani**, emphasized AABA AABA form, and eliminated suggestive elements.

A32. A **crossover hit** is a song that charts in **multiple categories** (Pop, R&B, C&W) at the same time. **Black appeal radio** and integrated jukebox listening meant R&B records started selling in Pop‑oriented record stores, so chart compilers saw the same songs succeeding across categories.

A33. **Moral panic** describes the overblown fear and anger among some white middle‑class adults that R&B and rock and roll would **corrupt youth** (sex, race mixing, rebellion). Parents objected to “race music,” sensual vocals, and dancing, and used these concerns to push bans and censorship.

A34. In this course, **Rock ’n’ Roll** is a style that blends **Pop, Country & Western, and R&B**, performed by both white and Black artists and targeted primarily at **teenagers**. The phrase “rock and roll” comes from **African American culture**, originally referring to having a party/good time (with sexual overtones) before DJs like Alan Freed applied it to the new music.

A35. **Bill Haley**, born **1925**, led Bill Haley and the Comets and was one of the first major rock and roll artists. But by **1955**, at about **30 years old**, he was considered **too old** to be the face of music for teenagers, who preferred artists closer to their own age.

A36. **“Rock Around the Clock”** (Bill Haley and the Comets, 1954; Freedman/Myers) uses **12‑bar blues** with up‑tempo energy, guitar‑driven timbre, and is designed for **fast dancing**. It only became a massive hit in **1955** when featured in the film **Blackboard Jungle**, where it was linked to images of rebellious youth.

A37. **Sam Phillips**, owner of **Sun Records** in Memphis, recorded both R&B (e.g., B.B. King) and C&W (e.g., Johnny Cash). He is famous for (reportedly) saying he wanted a **white man with the singing voice of a Black man**, meaning someone culturally shaped by African American music. He discovered Elvis and recognized the commercial potential of this cultural blend.

A38. **Elvis Presley** was born in **Tupelo, Mississippi**, near Memphis, and grew up attending both **white and Black churches**. He listened to **Dean Martin** (TPA/pop), **WDIA’s race music**, and church music. This mix of influences (Pop, R&B, gospel, C&W) helped create his hybrid style.

A39. At Elvis’s first Sun session in **July 1954**, Sam Phillips had him sing mainly **C&W**. During a break, Elvis started fooling around with the R&B song **“That’s Alright Mama”** (Arthur Crudup). Sam heard the energy and stylistic blend, realized this was the sound he’d been looking for, and turned it into Elvis’s first significant record.

A40. Bill Monroe’s original **“Blue Moon of Kentucky”** is a **bluegrass waltz in 3/4**, with a more emotionally distant, nasal vocal. Elvis’s 1954 version changes the **meter to 4/4**, adds slap bass and slap echo, sings the sad lyrics as if happy, and treats the **sound and rhythm as more important than the words**, turning it into a **pop/rockabilly** track.

A41. **Rockabilly** is Elvis’s early style blending **Rock ’n’ Roll** with **Hillbilly/C&W**. His nickname **“The Hillbilly Cat”** captures this mix: “Hillbilly” from C&W roots and “Cat” from Black slang for someone cool and in‑the‑know.

A42. Sam Phillips sold Elvis’s contract because Sun Records **lacked the capital** (on the order of **$10 million**) to press and distribute enough records nationally. **RCA** and **Colonel Tom Parker** paid **$35,000**, then in **1956** Parker put Elvis on **national TV 11 times**, making him a household name.

A43. **“Heartbreak Hotel”** (1956) reached **#1 Pop, #5 R&B**. Later that year, **“Hound Dog”** and **“Don’t Be Cruel”** were both **simultaneously #1 on Pop, Country, and R&B** for a brief period. These achievements show the **collapse of racial and stylistic boundaries** in the charts and the commercial power of rock and roll.

A44. On **Texaco Star Theatre** (June 1956), Elvis’s wild performance of **“Hound Dog”** ran over time, confused the band, and generated about **10,000 letters of outrage** accusing him of corrupting youth. Two weeks later, on **The Steve Allen Show**, he was forced into a **stiff, humiliatingly restrained** performance (e.g., singing to a basset hound) to calm moral panic—illustrating tension between youth culture and middle‑class values.

A45. Elvis’s trajectory: **Sun (1954–55)** – regional rockabilly star with Scotty Moore and Bill Black, no drummer at first. **RCA/TV breakthrough (1956–57)** – national stardom and multi‑chart hits. **Army (1958–1960)** – drafted, stationed in Germany, recordings pre‑released while away. **Hollywood era (early–mid 1960s)** – many formulaic movies and soundtrack albums, declining artistic quality. **1968 Comeback Special** – TV variety show re‑centering him as a live performer (opens with “That’s Alright Mama”). **Las Vegas residency (1970–77)** – mega‑shows, growing drug addiction and health decline. He died **August 16, 1977**, age **42**, of a drug overdose.

A46. **Chuck Berry** was born **October 18, 1926** in **St. Louis** to a **middle‑class** family. Unlike Elvis, whose primary identity was as a singer, Berry was **always holding a guitar** and defined himself as a **guitarist and songwriter**. His explicit goal was to **become rich**, and he was extremely **self‑conscious** about tailoring his music to appeal to middle‑class white audiences.

A47. At **Chess Records** (signed July 1955), Berry rewrote the C&W song **“Ida Red”** into **“Maybellene”**, changing the lyrics and theme while keeping some musical roots. The Chess brothers told him that writing his own songs would allow him to keep **all the royalties**, so he rewrote the piece and turned it into a **#1 R&B, #5 Pop** hit, demonstrating both **business savvy** and a new kind of self‑authored rock and roll.

A48. The three themes are:  
– **Cars**: symbols of freedom and mobility.  
– **Girls**: women treated as objects to be pursued/acquired.  
– **No school**: skipping class, rebellion against educational and adult authority.  
Chuck Berry’s songs frequently reference all three (e.g., cars and school in “Johnny B. Goode” and other tracks).

A49. In **“Johnny B. Goode” (1958)**:  
**White elements** – text‑heavy narrative lyrics, **clear enunciation**, and a **country‑influenced guitar tone**.  
**Black elements** – **12‑bar blues** harmony, boogie‑woogie‑style **added 6th** in the rhythm part, and a guitar solo focused on **timbre, articulation, and rhythm**, including playing the same pitch in different positions to change timbre.  
Together they made Berry uniquely appealing across racial lines.

A50. 1960s bands like the **Beatles** modeled themselves on Chuck Berry because he **wrote his own songs**, **played lead guitar**, and fronted a **self‑contained band**, fitting the emerging rock band ideal. Elvis, by contrast, rarely wrote material, didn’t always play guitar, and fit the older **division‑of‑labour** system where the singer was separate from songwriters and backing musicians.

A51. The **Golden Age of Rock and Roll (1954–1959)** is defined by:  
1) **Revenue**: US record industry worth about **$200M/year in 1954**, rising to **$600M/year by 1959**.  
2) **Pop Top 10 share**: Rock and roll records at **15% of Top 10 in 1955**, rising to **42% by 1959**.  
3) **Independent label share**: independents’ market share going from **21% in 1955** to **66% by 1959**.

A52. **Payola** = paying DJs under the table to play certain records. It had existed since the 1920s, but in the late 1950s **major labels and politicians** used the **Payola hearings** to portray rock and roll as **corrupt**, implicitly associating it with Blackness and youth. **Alan Freed**, a prominent rock and roll DJ, was targeted: he was charged, blacklisted, lost his job and marriage, went bankrupt, and died in the mid‑1960s.

A53. Opposition to rock and roll overlapped with **resistance to Civil Rights**. The style emerged alongside events like **Brown v. Board of Education (1954)**, **Rosa Parks and the Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955)**, and **the Little Rock Nine (1957)**, as well as Freedom Rides and lunch‑counter sit‑ins. Many white middle‑class adults saw both **racial integration** and Black‑influenced music as threats, so **dislike of rock ’n’ roll** became intertwined with **racist fears**.

A54. The **Great Extinction (1957–1959)** refers to the removal of major rock and roll stars from the scene:  
– **Elvis** – drafted into the **army** (March 1958).  
– **Chuck Berry** – arrested December 1959 under the **Mann Act** for transporting a young woman across a state line to work at his club.  
– **Jerry Lee Lewis** – career collapsed in 1958 when his marriage to his **13‑year‑old cousin** became public (despite hits like “Great Balls of Fire”).  
– **Little Richard** – after a plane engine incident in 1957, he experienced a religious crisis, became a **born‑again minister**, and left rock and roll.  
– **Buddy Holly** – died in a 1959 plane crash with **Ritchie Valens** and **The Big Bopper**, later memorialized as **“the day the music died.”**

A55. The **In‑Between Years (1959–1963)** lie between the end of the Golden Age of rock and roll and the arrival of the **Beatles** (Feb 1964). With many rock pioneers gone, **major labels** accepted that rock‑influenced music was here to stay but steered it into more controllable sub‑genres that fit the **division‑of‑labour** model, such as the **Dance Craze** and **Teen Idols**.

A56. The **Dance Craze** centered on songs like **“The Twist”** (Chubby Checker, written by Hank Ballard) and **“The Locomotion”** (Little Eva, written by Goffin/Carole King). These featured **professional studio bands**, heavy background vocals, and tightly controlled production. Songs were written by professionals for teenage dancers, with performers slotted into pre‑made material.

A57. **Teen Idols** were **clean‑cut, idealized boyfriend figures** marketed primarily to teenage girls and their parents. Songs like **“Blue Velvet”** (Bobby Vinton, 1963) featured non‑sexualized romance, smooth crooning, and lush arrangements. Although it sounded contemporary with electric guitar touches, it was actually a **TPA song from 1950**, bringing teens back to older Pop aesthetics.

A58. The **Brill Building** was a cluster of music businesses in New York where whole floors were dedicated to songwriting and publishing. **Aldon Music**, run by **Don Kirshner and Al Nevins**, employed elite writing teams like **Gerry Goffin & Carole King** and Neil Diamond. Like TPA, it used **division of labour** and produced polished, commercial songs (e.g., **“You’ve Lost That Lovin’ Feeling”**).

A59. **Jack Mullin**, a US Army engineer, discovered the German **Magnetophone**, the first high‑quality tape‑to‑tape recorder. **Bing Crosby** embraced it to **pre‑record** his weekly radio show instead of performing live. Tape allowed **editing** (cutting mistakes, splicing takes), and Crosby invested in tape technology, helping create **AMPEX**, then gave an early reel‑to‑reel machine to **Les Paul**.

A60. **Les Paul (1915–2009)** contributed:  
1) The **solid‑body electric guitar** (“The Log”), later commercialized as the **Gibson Les Paul**, solving acoustic guitars’ quick sound decay and enabling sustained, amplified tones.  
2) **Multitrack recording**, by splitting tape into multiple independent tracks so instruments and vocals could be recorded separately and layered.

A61. **Multitrack recording** means recording multiple separate audio tracks that can be **balanced, edited, and processed independently** before mixing. It turned recording from **reproduction** (capturing a live performance in one take) into **production** (constructing performances piece by piece over days or weeks), as heard in **Les Paul & Mary Ford’s “Sitting on Top of the World.”**

A62. Traditionally, a **producer** just found musicians and songs, rehearsed them, and supervised a live recording. In the tape and multitrack era, producers became creative architects of sound—choosing arrangements, layering tracks, and shaping tone and space. **Phil Spector’s “Wall of Sound”** used large ensembles recorded together, then mixed into a dense “musical stew” where individual instruments blur, with vocals added last.

A63. **Verse/chorus** form has verses with **same music but different words** and choruses with **same music and same words** (often including the title) and increased intensity. **AABA** centres the title in the first line and uses a single contrasting B section. In the in‑between years, **verse/chorus** (e.g., **“Be My Baby”** by the Ronettes) became more dominant than AABA.

A64. **Girl Groups** and **Boy Bands** are ensembles where **everyone is a vocalist and choreographer**, not instrumentalists. They are products of Brill‑style, division‑of‑labour systems: professional songwriters and studio musicians create the tracks; the groups perform vocals and choreography, cycling in and out of fashion over time (e.g., the Ronettes).

A65. **Surf Music** is rock and roll filtered through **West Coast youth culture**, especially surfing. It features **solid‑body electric guitars** and **electric bass** front and centre, often with instrumental tracks, and is one of the few post‑war styles where the band (rather than the vocalist alone) returns to the foreground.

A66. **Dick Dale** is a pioneer of **instrumental surf** (e.g., **“Misirlou”**), using aggressive, reverb‑heavy electric guitar. **The Beach Boys** (originally **The Pendletones**) are the quintessential surf band; their **“Surfin’ USA”** borrows heavily from Chuck Berry’s **“Sweet Little Sixteen”** (stop‑time, similar guitar riff), showing Berry’s deep influence. Chuck ultimately received songwriting credit and was pleased by both influence and royalties.

A67. **Brian Wilson** (tall bassist in the Beach Boys) was the band’s main **creative force**—a gifted multi‑instrumentalist with strong **music theory** and arranging skills. He also acted as **producer**, using multitrack recording and sophisticated voice‑leading (e.g., on “Good Vibrations”), earning admiration from peers like the Beatles.

A68. Post‑war Britain suffered heavy **bombing**, slow economic recovery, and **unemployment**, especially among youth. With few formal entertainment venues, a **DIY culture** emerged: teenagers formed **skiffle** bands using whatever instruments were available. At the same time, **American records** arrived via US troops and black markets, seeding R&B and rock influences that eventually fed into bands like the Beatles.

A69. **Skiffle** is a DIY style where young musicians perform simplified versions of American R&B and folk songs using **cheap or improvised instruments** (acoustic guitars, wash‑tub bass, makeshift drums). **Lonnie Donegan’s** version of **“Rock Island Line”** turned a Lead Belly song into a UK skiffle hit, inspiring many future rock musicians.

A70. The Beatles began as **The Quarrymen** in **Liverpool (1957)**, playing skiffle/R&B. With **John Lennon and Paul McCartney** writing original songs, **George Harrison** joining as lead guitarist (1958), and later drummer **Pete Best**, they honed their craft in Liverpool and **Hamburg** nightclubs. By **late 1961** they were fixtures at the **Cavern Club**, where manager **Brian Epstein** discovered them and worked to secure a record deal.

A71. **DECA Records** rejected the Beatles around **1962**, claiming there was “no future in guitar‑based music,” reflecting the dominance of **Teen Idols and producer‑driven pop** at the time. This misjudgment shows how out of step major labels could be with emerging band‑based rock trends.

A72. **George Martin**, a producer at **Parlophone (EMI)**, had extensive experience with **comedy and novelty records**, which required creative sound design, editing, and musical versatility. He brought these skills to the Beatles, building sonic “worlds” in the studio and helping translate their ideas into sophisticated recordings.

A73. In **“Please Please Me” (1963)**, the Beatles use **AABA form**, increase **lyric and rhythmic density** in key spots, and place the **highest vocal note at the end of the B section**, creating tension that resolves when A returns. Numerous small, rehearsed details (backing vocals, rhythmic accents) show their high level of preparation and musical craft.
