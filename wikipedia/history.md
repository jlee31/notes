# Alan Turing and the Turing Machine

Before the 1920s, a computer referred to a person who performed calculations, often using paper and pencil. Many of these human computers were women who worked as clerks carrying out mathematical computations.

The Church–Turing thesis proposed that any computation that can be performed by a human following a precise, step-by-step method (an algorithm) with paper and pencil can also be performed by a machine.

This type of calculation is considered mechanical, meaning it follows a fixed set of rules without requiring creativity or insight. Over time, these mechanical procedures gradually moved from being performed by humans to being executed by digital machines.

The Turing Machine, introduced by Alan Turing, is a hypothetical device used to study the capabilities and limitations of such computing processes.

The mathematical foundation of modern CS started with Kurt Godel with the incompleteness theorem. Within a formal system, he showed what was possible and impossible. Future formal systems are mu-recursive functions or lambda-definable functions.

In 1936 Alan Turing and Alonzo Church independently, and also together, introduced the formalization of an algorithm. 
Church–Turing thesis states that an algorithm created on a computer can perform any calculation given enough time and storage space. 

The Turing machine is a mathematical model of computation describing an abstract machine.
